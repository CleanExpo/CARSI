## Module 1: The WHS law that applies to you in Australia

> **EDUCATIONAL & PRE-TRAINING USE ONLY — NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Best-practice educational summary from published research and public regulator guidance, current to July 2026. Not Australian or New Zealand legal advice or representation. Verify against current law and your state/territory WHS regulator before acting.

### How work health and safety is governed in Australia

If you have done a US-style safety course, most of the framework you learned does not apply here. Australia has no OSHA and no single national safety code. Instead we have work health and safety (WHS) law.

Safe Work Australia writes the model WHS Act, model WHS Regulations and model Codes of Practice — but it is a policy body only. It does not inspect, issue notices or prosecute. Enforcement is done by your state or territory regulator: SafeWork NSW, WorkSafe Victoria, Workplace Health and Safety Queensland, WorkSafe WA, SafeWork SA, WorkSafe Tasmania, WorkSafe ACT, NT WorkSafe, or Comcare for national employers.

The model laws have been adopted by every jurisdiction except Victoria (Western Australia was the last to harmonise, on 31 March 2022). Victoria runs its own Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004, enforced by WorkSafe Victoria, and still uses “employer / employee” language rather than the WHS terms below.

> Golden rule for a mobile restoration crew: the duties are similar everywhere, but the Act, the section numbers and the reporting line change at the border. Always check the regulator in the state you are working in.

### Who owes what duty — PCBU, officers and workers

Under the model WHS Act the primary duty holder is the PCBU — a Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking. This replaces the old “employer” and is broader; “worker” likewise covers employees, contractors, labour hire and volunteers.

- PCBU — primary duty of care (s.19): ensure health and safety so far as is reasonably practicable (SFAIRP).
- Officers — due diligence (s.27): directors and senior managers must take proactive, reasonable steps to ensure the PCBU complies. This is a personal, prosecutable duty.
- Workers: take reasonable care for your own and others’ safety, follow reasonable instructions and cooperate with policies.

Industrial manslaughter is now an offence in every Australian jurisdiction (Queensland led in 2017; NSW commenced 16 September 2024 and Tasmania on 2 October 2024 completed national coverage). Gross negligence by a PCBU or officer causing a worker’s death carries penalties into the tens of millions of dollars and years of imprisonment.

Psychosocial risk (bullying, violence, harassment, unreasonable job demands) is also a legal WHS duty nationally, with Victoria’s standalone regulations commencing 1 December 2025 — it is not just “wellbeing”.

Terms you will use daily: SWMS (Safe Work Method Statement), JSEA (Job Safety and Environmental Analysis), site induction, White Card (general construction induction, unit CPCWHS1001), and RCD / safety switch.

## Module 2: Finding and controlling hazards — the risk management process

> **EDUCATIONAL & PRE-TRAINING USE ONLY — NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Best-practice educational summary from published research and public regulator guidance, current to July 2026. Not Australian or New Zealand legal advice or representation. Verify against current law and your state/territory WHS regulator before acting.

### Identify, assess, control, review — and the hierarchy of controls

Every WHS duty comes down to one repeatable loop you run on every job:

- Identify the hazards (walk the site; think chemical, biological, electrical, physical, psychosocial).
- Assess the risk (how likely, how severe).
- Control the risk using the hierarchy below.
- Review the controls — did they work, did the site change?

The hierarchy of controls is worked top down. PPE is the last line, never the first:

- Elimination — remove the hazard (e.g. don’t dry-cut; don’t enter until isolated).
- Substitution — a safer product or method.
- Isolation — separate people from the hazard (containment, barricades).
- Engineering — water suppression, on-tool dust extraction, negative air.
- Administrative — procedures, training, signage, rotation.
- PPE — respirators, gloves, coveralls (protects one person, only if worn and fitted correctly).

> “Reasonably practicable” (SFAIRP) means you weigh the risk against what it takes to control it — but the higher the harm, the more you must do.

### SWMS, JSEA and site induction on a restoration job

A Safe Work Method Statement (SWMS) is legally mandatory before you start high-risk construction work. There are 18 defined categories; the ones restoration crews meet most are work where a person could fall more than 2 m, asbestos disturbance, confined spaces, demolition, and work on or near energised electrical installations. The SWMS names the hazards, the controls and how the work is done, in consultation with the workers doing it.

A JSEA (Job Safety and Environmental Analysis) is a task-by-task risk assessment. It is good practice but not statutory like a SWMS — use it for the many restoration tasks that fall outside high-risk construction work.

Before working on a construction-type site you also need a site-specific induction and, for construction work, a White Card (nationally recognised, valid in every state and territory).

## Module 3: Hazardous materials on restoration and strip-out sites

> **EDUCATIONAL & PRE-TRAINING USE ONLY — NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Best-practice educational summary from published research and public regulator guidance, current to July 2026. Not Australian or New Zealand legal advice or representation. Verify against current law and your state/territory WHS regulator before acting.

### Asbestos — assume it in any pre-2004 building

Australia has had a total ban on manufacturing, supplying and using asbestos since 31 December 2003, so all asbestos work today is legacy removal and management. Any workplace or building constructed before 31 December 2003 must have an asbestos register, and where asbestos is (or is assumed) present, an asbestos management plan.

The rule that protects you: if a material cannot be tested, assume it is asbestos. On a restoration job, be especially careful with cement sheet, vinyl and lino, textured “popcorn” ceilings, pipe lagging, eaves and roofing in older buildings — and remember fire and water damage can turn bonded (non-friable) asbestos into friable asbestos. Check the register, or assume and test, before you cut, sand or demolish.

Removal licensing (model WHS Regulations):

- Class A licence — removes all asbestos, including friable asbestos and asbestos-contaminated dust.
- Class B licence — non-friable (bonded) asbestos only, required once you remove more than 10 m².
- No licence is needed only for 10 m² or less of non-friable asbestos — except in the ACT, where any amount needs a licence. Friable asbestos, any amount, always needs Class A.

Workers need training (asbestos awareness, or units CPCCDE3014 non-friable / CPCCDE3015 friable). The national awareness body is ASEA — the Asbestos and Silica Safety and Eradication Agency (renamed in December 2023 to add silica); note it is not a regulator — enforcement stays with your state WHS regulator.

### Silica and the engineered stone ban

Silica is the fastest-changing hazard in this trade. Two things you must know:

1. Engineered stone is banned. Since 1 July 2024 it is an offence for a PCBU to carry out (or allow) the manufacture, supply, processing or installation of engineered stone benchtops, panels and slabs, in every state and territory. Since 1 January 2025 it is also a prohibited import. Working with legacy engineered stone already installed — repair, minor modification, removal or disposal during a strip-out — is still permitted, but you must notify your WHS regulator before the work and use full silica controls.

2. All crystalline silica is controlled. From 1 September 2024, processing any crystalline silica substance (CSS) — any material with at least 1% crystalline silica, which includes concrete, brick, tile, mortar, grout, natural stone and fibre-cement — without controls is prohibited. If cutting, grinding, drilling or demolishing is assessed as high risk, the PCBU must prepare a written Silica Risk Control Plan before work starts, arrange air monitoring (report to the regulator if the standard is exceeded) and health monitoring, and provide silica-specific training.

The current workplace exposure standard for respirable crystalline silica is 0.05 mg/m³ (8-hour TWA). From 1 December 2026 the “workplace exposure standards (WES)” become “workplace exposure limits (WEL)”; the silica limit itself is under further review, so keep working to 0.05 mg/m³ and watch for the updated figure.

> Controls in order: engineering first — on-tool water suppression or H-class dust extraction — then RPE (minimum P2, correctly fit-tested) as the last line, never the only line.

### Lead, and chemicals under the GHS

Lead. Australian house paint could contain up to 50% lead until 1970 (cut to 0.1% only in December 1997), so pre-1970 homes very likely contain high-lead paint. Sanding, scraping, heat-gunning or demolishing painted surfaces makes lead dust and fume. “Lead-risk work” triggers notification to the regulator and health (blood-lead) monitoring. The removal-from-work blood-lead levels were lowered in 2025 to 20 µg/dL for males and females not of reproductive capacity (was 40) and 5 µg/dL for females of reproductive capacity (was 10) — any course still quoting 40/10 is out of date. Controls: assess, contain, wet methods, HEPA, P2 RPE, decontamination; never dry-sand or uncontrolled-blast.

Chemicals. Australia classifies and labels workplace chemicals under the Globally Harmonised System (GHS) Revision 7, mandatory since 1 January 2023. Practical duties on site:

- Use Safety Data Sheets (SDS) — not the old US term “MSDS” — kept current (reviewed at least every 5 years) and accessible to workers.
- Keep a register of hazardous chemicals used, handled or stored.
- Label every container to GHS 7, including anything you decant or site-mix.
- Placarding and a manifest to emergency services apply above the threshold quantities.

For any exposure or poisoning, Poisons Information is 13 11 26; emergencies are 000.

## Module 4: PPE and respiratory protection

### Choosing the right respirator — P1, P2, P3

Respiratory protection in Australia is governed by two standards: AS/NZS 1715 (selection, use and maintenance — how to run a respirator program) and AS/NZS 1716 (the device standard that defines the filter classes). Any respirator you use for these jobs must be marked to AS/NZS 1716.

- P1 — coarse mechanically generated dust only; rarely enough for restoration hazards.
- P2 — fine particulate, fumes and mists. This is the Australian baseline for mould and fire/soot clean-up. (P2 is broadly equivalent to the US “N95”, but in Australia you must specify P2.)
- P3 — highest particulate protection, but it only delivers its rated protection on a full-face or tight-fitting non-disposable respirator. Required for heavy mould loads inside containment, silica, and significant biohazard or Category 3 aerosols.

Fit-testing vs fit-checking. Any tight-fitting respirator (P2/P3) must be fit-tested to the individual before first use and periodically after, and you must be clean-shaven at the seal. A fit-check (user seal check) is done every single time you put it on — it does not replace fit-testing.

Quick mapping: mould inspection/light work — P2 (P3 full-face in active containment); soot and fire residue — P2 stepping to P3 full-face where combustion products or older materials are disturbed; silica — minimum P2, P3 for higher exposure; biohazard/Cat 3 — P2 floor, P3 full-face where spray or aerosol is likely, with eye and face protection.

### The rest of the kit — and a US→AU translation you can trust

Beyond the respirator, match the rest of the kit to the hazard: fluid-resistant coveralls, gloves (nitrile for chemical/biohazard), eye and face protection, and boots. PPE is issued, maintained and replaced by the PCBU at no cost to the worker.

## Module 5: Site-specific hazards on restoration jobs

> **EDUCATIONAL & PRE-TRAINING USE ONLY — NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Best-practice educational summary from published research and public regulator guidance, current to July 2026. Not Australian or New Zealand legal advice or representation. Verify against current law and your state/territory WHS regulator before acting.

### Electricity on a wet site

A flooded or stripped building is effectively a construction/demolition environment, so AS/NZS 3012 (electrical installations on construction and demolition sites) and AS/NZS 3760 (in-service testing — “test and tag”) apply to your temporary supply, leads and portable gear.

Australian mains is 230 V at 50 Hz. Water lowers your body’s resistance and creates earth paths, and a current as low as about 30 mA can cause a fatal heart rhythm — which is exactly why every portable tool and lead on a wet site must be protected by a residual current device (RCD / safety switch) rated at 30 mA or less, push-button tested before use.

> The property’s own RCDs and switchboard may be damaged by water ingress — do not assume site protection exists. Bring your own portable RCD, and confirm isolation (or have a licensed electrician confirm safe supply) before you enter standing water near energised circuits.

### Confined spaces, biohazard and mould

Confined spaces. Subfloors, crawl spaces, roof voids, tanks, pits and sumps become confined spaces after water damage — restricted entry, not built for occupancy, and prone to oxygen deficiency and hydrogen sulphide or methane from decomposing sewage. Work to AS 2865 and the Safe Work Australia Confined Spaces Code of Practice: risk assessment, entry permit, atmospheric testing (oxygen, flammables, toxics) before and during entry, ventilation, a standby person and a rescue plan. Never run petrol pumps or generators near a confined space — carbon monoxide is a classic restoration fatality.

Biohazard and Category 3 water. There is no single Australian “biohazard standard”; the duty is the general WHS risk-control duty plus state health infection-control practice (there is no local equivalent of the US Bloodborne Pathogens standard). Grossly contaminated (Category 3) water and sewage need full PPE, source control, no aerosolisation, decontamination and tracked waste. The IICRC S500 (water, with its Category 1/2/3 classes) and S540 (trauma) are the recognised industry methods you use to meet your Australian WHS duty — they are not Australian law.

Mould. Likewise there is no single Australian mould standard: the duty is met through WHS risk management, enHealth guidance and state health advice, with IICRC S520 as the recognised industry method. Control moisture first, then contain, then run HEPA-filtered negative air. Where mould is present, dry with air-filtration devices and dehumidifiers — do not run air movers, which aerosolise spores, until the area is contained and cleared to Condition 1.

### Heat, manual handling, falls and noise

Four everyday hazards, each with a Safe Work Australia Code of Practice or guide your state regulator adopts:

- Working in heat — PPE, coveralls, running drying equipment and un-air-conditioned damaged buildings add up fast. Manage hydration, work/rest cycles, acclimatisation and watch for heat illness.
- Hazardous manual tasks — dragging saturated carpet and underlay, awkward subfloor postures and repetitive extraction. Control force, repetition, posture and duration.
- Falls — roof-leak access, ladders, wet surfaces and elevated tanks. Manage any fall risk (historically emphasised above 2 m) using the fall-control hierarchy.
- Noise — banks of air movers, extraction and cutting tools. The exposure standard is 85 dB(A) averaged over 8 hours, with a 140 dB(C) peak; provide and fit hearing protection (to AS/NZS 1270) when it is exceeded.

## Module 6: Decontamination, zones and waste

> **EDUCATIONAL & PRE-TRAINING USE ONLY — NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Best-practice educational summary from published research and public regulator guidance, current to July 2026. Not Australian or New Zealand legal advice or representation. Verify against current law and your state/territory WHS regulator before acting.

### Clean and dirty zones, and doffing without cross-contaminating

On any biohazard, sewage or mould job, keep a clear split between the dirty (work) zone, a transition/decontamination zone, and the clean zone. Move people and equipment one way, and doff PPE in the correct order in the transition zone so contamination does not walk into clean areas or your vehicle.

Clean before you disinfect. Disinfectants do not work on soiled surfaces, and you must respect product-claim limits: any therapeutic or disinfection claim (e.g. “kills 99.9%”, “hospital grade”) is regulated by the TGA, and under Australian Consumer Law you must not repeat a claim you cannot substantiate. Use products as labelled.

Waste. Clinical and related (biohazard) waste is regulated at state EPA level — segregate, contain, label and dispose of it through a licensed pathway, and keep your records.

## Module 7: Emergencies, first aid and incident reporting

> **EDUCATIONAL & PRE-TRAINING USE ONLY — NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Best-practice educational summary from published research and public regulator guidance, current to July 2026. Not Australian or New Zealand legal advice or representation. Verify against current law and your state/territory WHS regulator before acting.

### First aid and site emergency planning

Follow the Safe Work Australia First aid in the workplace Code of Practice: enough trained first aiders and suitably stocked kits for the risk, plus task-specific additions restoration needs — eyewash for chemical and biohazard work, and sharps/biohazard spill provisions.

Every site needs an emergency plan under the WHS Regulations: response and evacuation, contact points, first-aid access, and provisions for that job’s specific hazards. For any confined-space entry a rescue plan is mandatory — no entry without it.

Know the numbers: 000 for emergencies, 13 11 26 for the Poisons Information Centre.

### Notifiable incidents — report, and preserve the site

Some incidents must be reported to your state or territory WHS regulator immediately (not to any federal body, and there is no “OSHA 300 log” here). The three notifiable categories are:

- the death of a person;
- a serious injury or illness — needing immediate in-patient hospital treatment, or immediate treatment for an amputation, serious head/eye/burn injury, degloving, spinal injury, loss of a bodily function or serious laceration, or medical treatment within 48 hours of exposure to a substance;
- a dangerous incident — a near-miss that exposed anyone to serious risk (uncontrolled electrical or energy release, collapse, fire or explosion, escape of a hazardous substance).

Two further duties: keep written records for at least 5 years, and preserve the incident site — do not disturb it until an inspector arrives or the regulator directs otherwise, except to help an injured person, remove a deceased person, make the site safe, or as directed by police or the regulator.

> In Victoria you report to WorkSafe Victoria under the OHS Act — same idea, different Act and section numbers.

### Your WHS professional statement

By completing this course you can state, honestly, that you work to the Australian WHS framework:

> I identify hazards and control them by the hierarchy of controls, with PPE as the last line. I know my duties as a worker and my PCBU’s primary duty of care. I treat any pre-2004 building as possibly containing asbestos, and I control crystalline silica. I select P2/P3 respiratory protection to AS/NZS 1716, fit-tested. I protect against 230 V electrical risk with an RCD on a wet site. I do not enter a confined space without a permit and a rescue plan. I report notifiable incidents to my state regulator and preserve the site. I make no product or health claim I cannot substantiate.
