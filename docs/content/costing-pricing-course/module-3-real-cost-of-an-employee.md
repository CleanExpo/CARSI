**Learning outcome:** calculate the real, fully-loaded cost of an employee — actual/award wage plus
super, leave loading, workers comp and (if applicable) payroll tax — and turn it into a cost per
**billable** hour using a realistic utilisation rate.

---

## 3.1 Why "what I pay them" is not what they cost

Ask most restoration owners what a technician costs and they'll tell you the hourly rate on the
payslip. That number is wrong — usually by 40–70% once you add everything the law and the calendar
force on top (Lucrature and Better Back Office AU charge-out calculators, 2026, both quantify this
gap). A technician is not an hourly cost. They are an **annual cost** you have to recover across the
**limited number of hours you can actually bill**.

There are two multipliers stacked here, and owners miss both:

1. **The on-cost multiplier** — super, leave loading, workers comp, payroll tax add ~20–30% on top of
   the wage.
2. **The utilisation divisor** — you pay for ~1,976 hours a year but you can only *bill* a fraction of
   them. Travel, quoting, monitoring runs between billable tasks, admin, training and downtime are all
   paid but not charged.

Get these two right and your whole pricing model becomes honest. Get them wrong and you'll be busy,
fully booked, and quietly losing money on every hour.

---

## 3.2 Step 1 — Start from the real wage

Don't start from the award minimum unless that's genuinely what you pay. The **Cleaning Services
Award 2020 [MA000022]** minimum for a Cleaning Services Employee Level 2 is **$26.70/hr** (full-time,
from the first full pay period on/after 1 July 2025 — Fair Work Ombudsman Pay Guide MA000022). But
skilled restoration technicians are typically paid **above** award. Use *your* actual pay rate.

> **First: confirm your award.** Most restoration cleaning/drying work sits under the Cleaning
> Services Award [MA000022]; some structural/reinstatement work falls under the Building & Construction
> General On-site Award [MA000020]. The correct award sets your legal minimum and some entitlements.
> Confirm yours with Fair Work. `[Verify — accountant/Fair Work.]`

**Worked example.** We'll cost a mid-level restoration technician paid **$35.00/hr** (above the L2
award floor — realistic for skilled drying work). Full-time = 38 ordinary hours/week × 52 weeks =
**1,976 paid hours/year**. Base annual wage = 1,976 × $35.00 = **$69,160**.

---

## 3.3 Step 2 — Add the on-costs

### Superannuation Guarantee — 12%
From 1 July 2025 the SG rate is **12%** of ordinary time earnings and stays at 12% for FY2026-27 (this
is the top of the legislated schedule — ATO "Super guarantee" key rates). Super is calculated on
ordinary hours (not overtime).

  Super = $69,160 × 12% = **$8,299.20/yr**

### Annual leave loading — 17.5% on 4 weeks
Under most modern awards / Fair Work Act s90, employees get 17.5% leave loading on their 4 weeks of
annual leave. Four weeks = 152 ordinary hours.

  Leave loading = 152 hrs × $35.00 × 17.5% = **$931.00/yr**

### Workers compensation — state + industry rate
Compulsory, priced as a % of wages, and it depends on **both your state and your industry
classification**. This is a real cost lever, not a fixed number. From `research-and-figures.md` §4:

  - NSW (icare), Cleaning Services non-Govt: **5.76%** of wages (2025-26)
  - WA (WorkCover WA), Building & Other Industrial Cleaning: **2.53%** (2025-26)

The *same* work costs more than double in NSW vs WA. Use **your** state scheme's rate for **your**
WIC/industry class (icare NSW, WorkSafe VIC, WorkCover QLD, RTWSA, WorkCover WA, etc.).

For this worked example we'll use the **NSW cleaning rate, 5.76%**:

  Workers comp = $69,160 × 5.76% = **$3,983.62/yr**

### Payroll tax — usually $0 for a small business
Payroll tax is a **state** tax that only applies once your **total** Australian wages exceed the state
threshold — e.g. **$1,200,000 in NSW** (Revenue NSW, 2025-26; see §5). A restoration business with a
handful of staff is almost always **under** the threshold and pays **$0**. We'll use **$0** here, but
flag it: as you grow past ~$1.2M in total wages you cross into payroll tax — a step-change cost to plan
for. `[Confirm your state threshold + grouping with your accountant.]`

  Payroll tax = **$0/yr** (under threshold)

### The fully-loaded annual cost

| Component | Amount | Source |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Base wage (1,976 hrs × $35.00) | $69,160.00 | Owner's actual rate |
| Super @ 12% | $8,299.20 | ATO, FY2025-26 |
| Leave loading @ 17.5% × 4 wks | $931.00 | Fair Work Act s90 |
| Workers comp @ 5.76% (NSW cleaning) | $3,983.62 | icare NSW 2025-26 |
| Payroll tax | $0.00 | Under NSW threshold |
| **Fully-loaded annual cost** | **$82,373.82** | |

That's an **on-cost of $13,213.82 on a $69,160 wage — about 19.1% on top**, and we haven't even
touched utilisation yet. (Cross-check: the FairWork Mate TCO tool shows a similar ~20% overhead on an
$80k base — §3.) If your workers-comp class is higher (e.g. NSW Building Exterior Cleaning at 9.69%) or
you're over the payroll-tax threshold, this climbs further.

Fully-loaded cost **per paid hour** = $82,373.82 ÷ 1,976 = **$41.69/hr**. But nobody bills every paid
hour — which is Step 3, and the one that actually decides whether you make money.

---

## 3.4 Step 3 — Utilisation: the hours you can actually bill

You pay for 1,976 hours. You can't bill all of them. Subtract:

**Paid but non-working (leave):**
| Leave (paid, not worked) | Hours |
| --- | --- |
| Annual leave (4 weeks) | 152 |
| Personal/carer's (sick) leave (~2 weeks) | 76 |
| Public holidays (~11 days) | 83.6 |
| **Total paid leave** | **≈ 311.6 hrs** |

Paid *working* hours = 1,976 − 311.6 = **≈ 1,664 hrs**.

**Working but non-billable** (the silent margin-killer — travel between jobs, quoting/estimating,
equipment monitoring runs, admin/paperwork, training, downtime between storm events). In restoration
this is *significant* — a tech doing drying monitoring drives a lot and quotes a lot. Assume 35% of
working hours are non-billable (realistic; adjust to your reality):

  Billable hours = 1,664 × (1 − 0.35) = **≈ 1,082 billable hrs/year**

**Utilisation = 1,082 ÷ 1,976 = ~55%.** This is the number owners get catastrophically wrong. Many
build their price assuming ~1,900 billable hours; the real figure is closer to **1,000–1,200**.

---

## 3.5 Step 4 — Cost per BILLABLE hour

This is the number that matters — the true labour cost you have to recover on every hour you charge:

  Cost per billable hour = **$82,373.82 ÷ 1,082 = $76.13/hr**

Read that against the payslip: you *pay* $35.00/hr, the employee *costs you* $76.13 for every hour you
can actually bill — **2.2× the wage.** If you're charging a client $75/hr for labour thinking you're
making money "because I only pay them $35", you are **losing money before overheads, before
equipment, before a cent of profit.**

### Sensitivity — utilisation is everything

| Non-billable % | Billable hrs | Utilisation | Cost / billable hr |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 25% | 1,248 | ~63% | $66.01 |
| 35% (example) | 1,082 | ~55% | $76.13 |
| 45% | 915 | ~46% | $90.00 |

A 20-point swing in non-billable time changes the true cost by ~$24/hr. This is why the calculator
forces you to be honest about utilisation and shows this sensitivity — guessing high here is how
"fully booked" businesses go broke.

## 3.6 Do this with your numbers

1. Enter your technician's **actual** hourly rate (not the award minimum, unless that's what you pay).
2. Add super (12%), leave loading (17.5% on 4 weeks), and **your state/industry workers-comp rate**.
3. Confirm whether you're over your state's payroll-tax threshold (usually not).
4. Subtract paid leave and — honestly — your non-billable hours to get billable hours.
5. Divide fully-loaded annual cost by billable hours = **your** true cost per billable hour.

The pricing calculator does this automatically and can pull your wage and on-cost totals straight from
your bookkeeping (Xero/QuickBooks/MYOB) — see `pricing-calculator-spec.md`. Carry your cost-per-billable-hour
into **Module 6**, where you add overheads and margin to reach the rate you must charge.

> **Take to your accountant:** your award/wage basis, your state workers-comp classification and rate,
> your payroll-tax position, and your utilisation assumption. These four drive everything downstream.
