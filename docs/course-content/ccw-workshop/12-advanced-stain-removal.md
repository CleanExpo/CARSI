## The Escalation Ladder — What Each Rung Actually Does

Escalation is not "try a stronger product". Each rung is a **different chemical mechanism**. Climb only
as far as the stain forces you, and **rinse between rungs** — a residue from rung 2 can neutralise rung 3.

| Rung | Mechanism | Use for | Watch-out |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Aqueous spotter | Dissolve / suspend | Water-soluble soils | Over-application → re-soiling |
| 2. pH shift (acid **or** alkaline) | Move the stain off its set point | Tannin (acid), general organic (alkaline) | Match to fibre fence |
| 3. Enzyme / protein digester | Break protein bonds | Blood, dairy, vomit, urine solids | Needs dwell + warmth; cool water first on blood |
| 4. Solvent (POG) | Dissolve non-polar soil | Oil, grease, gum, adhesive, ink | Ventilate; flammability on some products |
| 5. Oxidiser (H₂O₂ 3–12%) | Break the chromophore by adding oxygen | Set tannin, coffee, some dyes | Test on fibre; can lighten some carpets |
| 6. Reducing agent (sulphite/bisulphite) | Break the chromophore by removing oxygen | Red dye, cordial, some inks | Heat-assisted; never mix with the oxidiser |

> **The chromophore idea.** A "stain" is visible because a molecule (the *chromophore*) absorbs light.
> You do not have to remove the whole molecule — you only have to **break the part that absorbs light.**
> Oxidisers do that by adding oxygen; reducers by taking it away. That is why a dye stain that laughs at
> detergent surrenders to a reducing agent: you are not lifting it, you are switching its colour off.

## Problem-Stain Playbook

Cold-first and test-first apply to **every** entry. All chemistry stays inside the fibre fence
(Module 2/3); on wool, keep acid-side products at **pH ≥ 2.5** and heat low.

### Red dye — cordial, red "sports" drinks, some lollies *(the classic call-out)*
1. Blot; do **not** heat yet.
2. Aqueous spotter first — some fresh red is just water-soluble colour.
3. If it holds: apply a **reducing-agent red-dye remover**, cover with a **damp white towel**, and apply
   **gentle heat** (steam iron over the towel, or the wand on a low pass) in short bursts. Check every
   30–60 s.
4. Rinse-extract fully; re-inspect at 24 h. **Never leave a reducing agent in the fibre** — rinse it.

> **DID YOU KNOW · Heat is the reducing agent's accelerator, not the enemy.** On most stains the manual's
> "cold-first" rule holds. Red-dye reducers are the deliberate exception — they are *designed* to be
> heat-activated. The danger is heat on the **wrong** stain (it sets protein and tannin), so heat is the
> last rung, applied only once you have confirmed a synthetic dye stain on a heat-tolerant fibre.

### Aged pet urine — the three-year-old yellow *(covered in Module 5; the advanced move)*
Fresh urine ≈ pH 6; aged urine climbs to **pH 10–12** as bacteria convert urea to ammonia. Hitting it with
alkaline pre-spray adds alkalinity to an already-alkaline, dye-stripping substrate. **Acid-side first.**
Advanced additions:
- **Sub-surface is the real job.** Surface colour is a fraction of the deposit; the pad, underlay and
  sometimes the sub-floor hold the salts. Set customer expectation: surface treatment ≠ decontamination.
- **Urine salts are hygroscopic** — they pull moisture from humid air and the odour returns on the next
  wet day. This is why "the smell came back" — it never left the underlay.
- Escalate to an **enzyme / oxidiser urine treatment** with genuine dwell for odour; disclose that heavy
  contamination may need sub-floor sealing or carpet/underlay replacement (outside CCW clean scope →
  refer or quote as restoration).

### Coffee, tea, red wine, tomato — **tannin**
Acidic spotter (wool-compatible, low-alkalinity) → if residual, **oxidiser** (H₂O₂). Tannin is the
textbook oxidiser stain. Rinse between; never chase tannin with a strong alkaline (it sets and browns).

### Blood, egg, dairy, vomit — **protein**
**Cool water only** — heat cooks protein and sets it permanently. Enzyme / mild-alkaline digester with
dwell. Vomit is protein **+ acid + food dye** — treat in sequence: protein first, then dye.

### Oil, grease, tar, lipstick, chewing gum, adhesive — **solvent-soluble**
POG solvent. Gum: **freeze and shatter** or a citrus/gum-freeze solvent, then POG for residue. Ventilate;
some solvents are flammable — no ignition sources, RCD-protected leads only.

### Ink — depends on the ink
Ballpoint (oil-based) → solvent/POG. Water-based/gel → aqueous then reducer. Permanent marker → solvent,
expect partial only. **Test a hidden area** — some inks bleed wider under solvent before they lift.

### Rust — **acid, never alkaline**
A dedicated **oxalic/rust remover** (strong acid). Ventilate, glove up (nitrile), keep off calcareous
adjacent surfaces. Rinse thoroughly — leftover acid re-soils and can damage the fibre-dye.

### Turmeric / curry / mustard — **natural dye + oil**
Common in Australian kitchens and shared houses. Oil component → POG first; the yellow curcumin stain is a
**dye** → oxidiser, and it is famously stubborn. **Sunlight fades curcumin** — an honest "it will keep
fading over a week in daylight" is often the truest advice. Disclose it may not fully clear.

### Nail polish — solvent, with a warning
Acetone lifts polish but **dissolves acetate and some dyes and backings**. Test hard; on a doubtful fibre,
refer rather than risk a hole.

### Candle wax — heat-transfer, not chemistry
Scrape the surface, lay a white blotter/paper towel, warm with a low iron to **wick the wax up into the
paper**. Then POG any residue dye. Do not start with solvent — you will spread it.

### Bleach / colour-loss spots — this is *not* a stain
A bleach spot has **removed** colour, not added it — no cleaner will bring it back. This is **colour
repair** (IICRC CRT territory): re-dyeing the affected tufts. Recognise it, disclose it honestly, and
quote it as a repair or refer — never pretend cleaning will fix it.

## The Australian Spill Profile *(field context)*

You will meet these disproportionately in AU homes and workplaces: **red cordial** (kids' parties),
**coffee** (the national default), **red wine**, **turmeric/curry**, **pet urine** (high pet-ownership),
**fake-tan** (a dihydroxyacetone stain — oxidiser-responsive, seasonal), **grass/clay** on entry
carpets, and **sunscreen** oil transfer on upholstery and vehicle interiors in summer. Stock the
six-bottle kit plus a **rust remover**, a **red-dye reducer**, and an **oxidiser** and you cover the
overwhelming majority.

## Safety — Non-Negotiable Chemistry Rules *(reinforced)*

- **Never mix an acid spotter with chlorine bleach** — chlorine gas.
- **Never mix an oxidiser with a reducing agent** — they neutralise each other and can generate heat.
- **Rinse thoroughly between escalation rungs.**
- Nitrile gloves and eye protection for oxidisers, reducers, rust removers and solvents — every time.

---

## Three-Tier Take-Aways

**NEWBY** — Identify the class, test, blot, cold-first. Know that a bleach spot is colour-loss, not a
stain. Never mix acid with chlorine or oxidiser with reducer.

**INTERMEDIATE** — Climb the escalation ladder one rung at a time, rinsing between; know which stains are
oxidiser (tannin) vs reducer (red dye) and why heat is deliberate on red dye but forbidden on protein.

**PRO** — Read sub-surface contamination and set honest expectations; recognise colour-repair vs cleaning;
document a permanent dye stain for the customer's record and stop at the three-attempt line.
