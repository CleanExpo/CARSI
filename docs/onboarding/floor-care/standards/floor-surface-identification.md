# Floor Surface Identification Guide (Pillar 4)

**Purpose:** Help technicians correctly identify a floor surface on site, pick safe chemistry and method, and avoid causing damage that ruins a contract.

---

## Why this matters

The wrong chemical or method can permanently damage a floor in minutes — etched stone, stripped vinyl, swollen timber, dulled coatings. You cannot un-do it. Identify the surface first, confirm whether it is sealed, then choose the gentlest method that does the job.

**Golden rule:** Always test a small inconspicuous area first (a corner, under a fixture, inside a doorway), let it dwell and dry as it would in real use, and check for colour change, dulling, etching, softening or lifting before you treat the whole floor.

---

## Chemistry primer (read this first)

Cleaning chemistry is mostly about pH. The pH scale runs 0 (strong acid) to 14 (strong alkaline), with 7 neutral.

| Type | pH range | What it's for | Main risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Neutral** | ~6–8 | Daily/general cleaning, safe on most sealed and polished floors | Lowest risk; may not shift heavy soil or grease |
| **Alkaline** | ~8–14 | Grease, oil, heavy soil, traffic lane build-up, strippers | Can dull/etch some stone, attack finishes, dry out timber |
| **Acidic** | ~1–6 | Mineral/scale, grout haze, rust, efflorescence | Etches and ruins natural stone, terrazzo, concrete; corrodes |

Other products you'll meet:
- **Solvents** — dissolve adhesive, ink, paint, some sealers. Can soften/melt vinyl, rubber and some coatings. Ventilate; check SDS.
- **Strippers** — strong alkaline products that remove old finish/polish. Aggressive. Will damage unsealed or sensitive surfaces and any floor that should not be stripped.
- **Sealers** — penetrate or coat porous surfaces to reduce soil and moisture uptake. Surface must be clean, dry and compatible.
- **Polishes / coatings (finishes)** — sacrificial top layers (often on vinyl/VCT) that take wear and are buffed/recoated. Confirm a floor is finished before stripping or burnishing.

> Cure times, re-entry times, dilution rates and any slip-resistance claims come from the product **SDS/TDS** and the relevant standard (e.g. **AS 4663**, **HB 198**, **NCC**). Never quote these from memory or treat them as fixed.

**Porosity in plain terms:**
- **Porous** — soaks up liquid (unsealed concrete, unsealed stone, raw timber). Acts fast; needs sealing; over-wetting causes damage.
- **Semi-porous** — limited uptake (some tile, sealed stone, linoleum).
- **Non-porous** — sheds liquid (sealed/coated surfaces, glazed porcelain, epoxy). Tolerates more water but can be slippery when wet.

## Surfaces

### Vinyl (sheet vinyl / luxury vinyl)
- **Recognise:** Flexible plastic flooring, sheet or plank/tile, printed pattern under a wear layer. Slight give underfoot, often welded seams in sheet vinyl.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Many vinyl floors carry a factory or applied **finish/polish**. Confirm before stripping — water beads/sits on a finished floor; a worn finish looks dull and patchy.
- **Porosity:** Non-porous (finish layer); seams and edges can let water under.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral for daily; controlled alkaline for finish strip only when you've confirmed it's appropriate.
- **Avoid:** Solvents (soften/stain), abrasive pads on the print, harsh acids, excess water at seams.
- **Damage risk:** Stripping a floor that should not be stripped; melting/dulling from solvent; water creeping under seams and lifting the floor.

### VCT (vinyl composition tile)
- **Recognise:** Hard, rigid square tiles, often speckled, common in schools, hospitals, retail. Almost always finished with multiple coats of polish.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Bare VCT is porous and dull; finished VCT has a gloss film.
- **Porosity:** Porous when bare; non-porous once finished.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral daily; alkaline stripper for full strip-and-recoat cycles.
- **Avoid:** Leaving bare VCT unsealed; strong acids; over-wetting bare tile.
- **Damage risk:** Bare tile absorbs soil and stains permanently; wrong pad/burnish can mark the finish.

### Linoleum (real lino)
- **Recognise:** Natural product (linseed oil, cork, jute backing). Often confused with vinyl but is **alkali-sensitive**. Matte, warm feel, jute backing visible at cut edges.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Usually factory-finished; a worn finish exposes the soft lino.
- **Porosity:** Semi-porous.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral only for routine work.
- **Avoid:** Strong alkaline cleaners and strippers (they break down linseed oil), excess water, high heat.
- **Damage risk:** Alkaline/water damage causes drying, embrittlement, colour change and "browning".

### Rubber
- **Recognise:** Studded or smooth rubber flooring/matting, gyms, entry areas, stair treads. Matte, grippy, slight chemical smell.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Usually unsealed; some have a factory cure.
- **Porosity:** Semi-porous.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral; manufacturer-approved cleaners.
- **Avoid:** Solvents and strong alkalis/acids (degrade rubber), abrasive scrubbing, oily products.
- **Damage risk:** Swelling, softening, surface breakdown and permanent staining/discolouration.

### Concrete (polished, sealed, coated or bare)
- **Recognise:** Grey/aggregate slab. Can be bare (porous, dusty), sealed (slight sheen), polished (reflective, ground finish) or coated.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Water soaks straight into bare concrete; beads on sealed/coated.
- **Porosity:** Porous bare; semi/non-porous when sealed.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral for sealed/polished; mild alkaline for heavy soil on bare/sealed.
- **Avoid:** Acids on polished/sealed concrete (etch and dull), strong acids generally.
- **Damage risk:** Etching, loss of polish/sheen, sealer breakdown, dusting if left bare.

### Terrazzo
- **Recognise:** Polished composite of stone/marble chips in cement or resin matrix. Decorative, common in older public buildings and foyers.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Often sealed and polished; dull patches show wear.
- **Porosity:** Semi-porous (cement-based especially).
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral only.
- **Avoid:** **Acids** (etch the marble chips and cement matrix), strong alkalis, abrasives.
- **Damage risk:** Acid etching and loss of polish — costly to restore.

### Ceramic & porcelain tile
- **Recognise:** Hard glazed (or matte) tiles with grout lines. Porcelain is denser and harder than ceramic.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Glazed bodies are non-porous; **grout** is usually porous unless sealed.
- **Porosity:** Tile non-porous; grout porous/semi-porous.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral daily; alkaline for grease; mild acid for grout haze/scale **only if grout and tile tolerate it** (test first).
- **Avoid:** Strong acids on coloured/sealed grout, abrasives on glaze.
- **Damage risk:** Acid attacks grout and any cement; abrasives scratch glaze; grout staining if left unsealed.

### Natural stone (marble, limestone, travertine, granite, slate, sandstone)
- **Recognise:** Real stone — veining, natural variation, cool to touch. Marble/limestone/travertine are calcium-based and soft; granite is harder.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Sealed stone resists water/oil staining; unsealed darkens when wet.
- **Porosity:** Porous to semi-porous.
- **Safe chemistry:** **Neutral, stone-safe products only.**
- **Avoid:** **Acids** (etch calcium-based stone almost instantly), strong alkalis, abrasives, vinegar/citrus/descalers.
- **Damage risk:** Acid etching, dull marks, staining, pitting — often unrepairable without professional restoration.

> **Natural stone is acid-sensitive. If unsure whether a hard floor is stone or look-alike tile, treat it as acid-sensitive until confirmed.**

### Timber & engineered timber
- **Recognise:** Solid boards or engineered planks (thin real-wood veneer over ply core). Grain, board joints, warm underfoot. Coated with oil, wax or polyurethane.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** Coated timber sheds water; unsealed/oiled timber absorbs it (darkens).
- **Porosity:** Porous to semi-porous depending on finish.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral, timber-specific products; barely-damp method.
- **Avoid:** **Water flooding, strippers, strong alkalis, steam, abrasives.**
- **Damage risk:** Swelling, cupping, board lift, white marks, finish damage from over-wetting; strippers destroy the coating and raise grain.

> **Timber is water- and stripper-sensitive. Keep it as dry as the job allows.**

### Epoxy / resin
- **Recognise:** Seamless poured coating, often glossy, common in kitchens, plant rooms, warehouses, labs. Can be flake/quartz-filled.
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** It is itself a coating; non-porous when intact, but worn/cracked areas let water in.
- **Porosity:** Non-porous (intact).
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral to mild alkaline.
- **Avoid:** Strong solvents (can soften/cloud some resins), aggressive acids, heavy abrasion.
- **Damage risk:** Clouding, softening, scratching the gloss, delamination at worn/cracked spots.

### Carpet & carpet tiles
- **Recognise:** Textile floor — broadloom or modular tiles. Fibre may be nylon, polypropylene, wool or wool-blend (wool needs gentler, lower-pH care).
- **Sealed vs unsealed:** N/A — consider stain protection/topical treatments instead.
- **Porosity:** Porous textile over backing/underlay.
- **Safe chemistry:** Neutral to mildly alkaline carpet products; correct dilution; control moisture.
- **Avoid:** High-pH/bleach products (browning, colour loss, especially wool), over-wetting (wicking, delamination, odour, slow drying), solvents on backing.
- **Damage risk:** Browning, colour bleed, shrinkage/delamination, mould and odour from trapped moisture.

## Quick reference table

| Surface | Porosity | Safe | Avoid | Main damage risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vinyl | Non-porous (finish) | Neutral; controlled strip | Solvents, acids, abrasives, edge water | Melting/dulling, lifting at seams |
| VCT | Porous bare / non-porous finished | Neutral; alkaline strip | Strong acids, over-wet bare tile | Permanent staining if bare |
| Linoleum | Semi-porous | Neutral | Strong alkali, water, heat | Browning, embrittlement |
| Rubber | Semi-porous | Neutral / approved | Solvents, strong acid/alkali | Swelling, breakdown, staining |
| Concrete | Porous bare / non-porous sealed | Neutral; mild alkaline | Acids on polished/sealed | Etching, sealer loss, dusting |
| Terrazzo | Semi-porous | Neutral | Acids, strong alkali, abrasives | Acid etching, loss of polish |
| Ceramic/porcelain | Tile non-porous; grout porous | Neutral; alkaline grease; mild acid (test) | Strong acid on grout, abrasives on glaze | Grout/grout-line damage, scratching |
| Natural stone | Porous–semi-porous | Neutral, stone-safe only | **Acids**, alkali, abrasives, citrus | Etching, staining, pitting |
| Timber / engineered | Porous–semi-porous | Neutral, timber-specific, damp | **Water flooding, strippers, steam** | Swelling, cupping, finish loss |
| Epoxy / resin | Non-porous (intact) | Neutral / mild alkaline | Strong solvents, acids, abrasion | Clouding, softening, scratching |
| Carpet / tiles | Porous textile | Neutral–mild alkaline, controlled moisture | High pH/bleach, over-wetting | Browning, shrinkage, mould/odour |

## On-site identification checklist

- [ ] Surface identified (or treated as the most sensitive likely option)
- [ ] Confirmed sealed / finished / coated vs bare
- [ ] Porosity assessed (water-drop test where appropriate)
- [ ] Chemistry selected per SDS/TDS at correct dilution
- [ ] Method matched to surface (pad/brush/moisture level)
- [ ] **Tested a small inconspicuous area and checked it dried clean with no damage**
- [ ] Cure / re-entry / drying times read from SDS/TDS and signage/barriers planned

> If you cannot identify a surface or confirm whether it's sealed, stop and check with your supervisor or the site before treating it.
