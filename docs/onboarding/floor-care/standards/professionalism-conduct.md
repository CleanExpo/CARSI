# Professionalism & Site Presentation Standard (Pillar 6)

**Purpose:** Set the standard for how we present ourselves and run our work on a client's site, so we earn trust and keep contracts.

---

## Why this matters

On most sites you are the only contact the client sees. To them, you *are* the company. A tidy, polite, organised crew gets invited back. A messy, careless or rude one loses the contract — no matter how good the floor looks at the end. Professionalism is not extra; it is the job.

- [ ] Company ID/lanyard visible where required — **[COMPANY TO CONFIRM]**
- [ ] Correct PPE on and serviceable (footwear, gloves, eye protection as the task/SDS requires)
- [ ] Footwear clean and appropriate; no thongs/open shoes
- [ ] No offensive slogans, no non-company branding over the top of the uniform

**Do:** turn up looking like a professional tradesperson.
**Not that:** mixed civvies, dirty hi-vis, uniform tied around the waist.

## Personal hygiene

- Clean, presentable, no strong body odour
- Hands and nails clean; hair tidy/contained as the task requires
- Cover coughs/sneezes; stay home and notify your supervisor if you're unwell, especially on childcare/school/healthcare sites
- Mind strong smells (smoke, food) around children and the public

## Behaviour & language on site

**Do:**
- Keep language clean and calm at all times — assume children and clients can hear you
- Be polite, brief and helpful when spoken to
- Follow the site's rules, sign-in process and induction
- Stay in your work area; only go where the job requires

**Not that:**
- Swearing, shouting, banter that carries across a school or office
- Loud music or phone calls on speaker
- Personal phone use in front of the client
- Arguing with staff, other contractors or the public — raise it with your supervisor instead

## Respect for people on site

You will work around children, teachers, office staff, tenants, visitors and other contractors. Treat all of them with courtesy.

- Give way to staff and the public; never make them step over your gear
- Be warm but professional with children — never single a child out, never go off alone with one (see Pillar 7)
- Acknowledge teachers/facility staff and follow their direction about timing and access
- Respect tenants' space and quiet; knock/announce where appropriate
- Never comment on, photograph or repeat anything private you see or hear

## Vehicle: cleanliness, parking & first impression

- Keep the vehicle clean and presentable — it carries the company name
- Park where directed; never block fire exits, accessible bays, gates, loading zones or pedestrian paths
- Reverse-park for a safe, quick exit where you can
- No idling outside classrooms/windows; mind fumes and noise
- Secure the vehicle and lock the chemicals/tools when unattended

## Setting up the work zone

**Unloading equipment**
- Unload close to the work area by the route agreed at sign-in, not through busy thoroughfares at peak times
- Use the trolley/path the site nominates; protect lifts, doors and walls

**Placing chemicals**
- Keep chemicals together in a controlled spot, away from children, food areas and drains
- Bunded/contained where required; lids on; SDS accessible
- Never leave chemicals unattended in reach of the public or children

**Hoses & leads**
- Run hoses and leads along walls/edges, not across walkways or doorways
- Use cable covers/ramps or tape down where crossing a path is unavoidable
- Flag wet areas with signage; keep exits clear at all times
- Coil and stow leads/hoses the moment a zone is finished

## Controlling the work zone without disrupting the site

- Barrier or sign off only what you need, for as long as you need
- Keep at least one clear path through for staff and the public
- Work around the site's timetable — avoid pick-up/drop-off, lunch, exams, meetings where possible
- Keep noise to the agreed windows
- Re-open areas as soon as they're safe and dry, don't hold them longer than needed

## Communicating delays, risks and issues early

The fastest way to lose trust is a surprise. Tell the client *before* it becomes their problem.

- Running late? Call ahead, give a realistic new time
- Found damage, a hazard, or an area you can't safely treat? Report it straight away with a photo only **of the floor/area, never of people** (see Pillar 7)
- Job will take longer or can't finish today? Flag it early and agree a plan
- Unsure about a surface or chemical? Stop and check — don't guess
- Log issues to your supervisor so the company can manage the relationship

## Why professionalism keeps contracts

Clients renew with crews they trust. Trust is built from small, visible things: arriving on time, looking the part, keeping their site tidy, not disrupting their day, telling them the truth early. Every site visit either strengthens or weakens that trust. Treat each one as the reason we get the next contract.

## Arrival impression sequence

1. Park correctly — clear of exits, bays and paths
2. Uniform/PPE on and tidy before you step out
3. Sign in / report to reception per site rules — **[COMPANY TO CONFIRM sign-in app/process]**
4. Confirm scope, access and timing with the site contact
5. Identify the agreed unload point, chemical spot and hose/lead route
6. Walk the area, note hazards and existing damage, raise anything unexpected
7. Set up tidily, barrier only what's needed, then start
