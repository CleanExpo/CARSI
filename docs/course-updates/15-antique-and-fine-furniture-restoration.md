## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "The over-drying trap: how the mitigation ruins the antique"
> Here's the counter-intuitive truth every contents tech should carry: on a water loss, the antique often
> survives the *water* and is destroyed by the *drying.* The structural-drying gear you (rightly) place to
> pull moisture out of the walls is aggressive — and it pulls water out of solid timber far faster than
> timber can safely give it up. The result is the classic damage: cracks, warping, joints springing loose,
> veneer lifting. The fix is simple and it's a decision, not a technique: **get the valuable pieces out of
> the drying zone** and into a **dry, climate-controlled space** while the building is dried hard. Don't
> leave them in the house to over-dry, and don't seal them in a pod where they'll stay wet and mould.

### 3b. New section — "Reversibility: the conservator's first rule"
> Professional conservation is governed by **reversibility** — the principle that what you add should be
> removable, and what you do should be re-treatable later. It shapes every decision, and it has one hard
> edge: **cleaning is not reversible.** Whatever you strip, sand or wash away cannot be put back. So before
> you clean an antique, be certain the material you're removing is *not original* to the piece — a finish,
> a patina, a maker's layer. On an antique, **restoration beats refinishing** and preserving the original
> finish preserves the value; the instinct to make it look new is exactly the instinct that destroys worth.

### 3c. New section — "Bound water vs free water (why the swings do the damage)"
> Wood carries water two ways. **Free water** sits in the hollow cell cavities and, coming and going, does
> little. **Bound water** is held inside the cell walls themselves — and *changes in bound water* drive
> nearly all of wood's shrinking and swelling. That's the key insight: it isn't the soaking that wrecks the
> joinery, it's the **change in moisture content.** Wood swells and forces mortise-and-tenon joints apart,
> then shrinks and leaves them loose; the glue, fatigued by the cycle, lets go. Control the *rate and range*
> of moisture change and you prevent most of the structural damage.

### 3d. New section — "Veneer, glue and blooming"
> - **Veneer fails at the core.** A veneered top is a thin skin over plywood, particleboard or MDF. Solid
>   wood that swells can usually be re-glued and straightened; a **particleboard core that swells is
>   permanently ruined**, and the veneer with it. Knowing the substrate tells you salvageable from
>   write-off before you quote.
> - **"Blooming."** That cloudy white film on a damp finish looks fatal but often isn't — a mild solvent
>   (turpentine, a dilute ammonia-and-water wipe, or oil worked with fine steel wool) will frequently clear
>   it, saving a full strip-and-refinish. Try to lift the bloom before you decide the finish is gone.
> - **The 20% line.** Mould keeps growing while wood sits above ~20% moisture content — don't return a
>   piece to use, and don't seal it, until it's dried below that and stabilised.

### 3e. Environment box
> - **Stable ~45–55% RH** — consistency matters more than the exact number; fluctuation is what fatigues
>   glue and veneer.
> - **Away from vents, direct sun and exterior walls** — those are the spots where temperature and humidity
>   swing hardest.
> - **Slow, even drying** — never in the sun; ventilate; expect weeks to months for a saturated piece.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> An antique can come through a flood and then be destroyed by the people trying to save the house. The
> mitigation crew's drying equipment is built to strip water out of a building *fast* — and it does the
> same to a nearby antique, pulling moisture from the timber so quickly the joints crack and the veneer
> lifts. Often the smartest thing you can do for a valuable piece after a flood is carry it *out* of the
> room being dried.
