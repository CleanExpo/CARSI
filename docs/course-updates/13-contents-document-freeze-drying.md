## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Two ways things dry: evaporation vs sublimation"
> Every wet material dries by one of two physical routes, and the difference decides how much damage you're
> left with:
> - **Evaporation** — liquid water turns to vapour. To leave the page, that water is pulled to the surface
>   by capillary action, and it drags dissolved ink and dirt with it. That's the mechanism behind
>   **tidelines, cockling (distortion), ink bleeding and "blocking"** (pages fusing together).
> - **Sublimation** — frozen water (ice) turns straight to vapour **without ever becoming liquid again.**
>   No liquid means no wicking, so the ink can't run, the fibres don't swell, and no new moisture is added.
>
> That's the whole reason freeze-drying exists: by keeping the water frozen the entire time, it removes it
> while skipping the one state — liquid — that does the damage.

### 3b. New section — "Freezing isn't drying — it's buying time"
> The single most useful first move on a large water-damaged paper loss is to **freeze it.** Not because
> freezing dries anything (it doesn't), but because it **stops the clock.** Freezing:
> - **arrests further damage** — no more bleeding, blocking or distortion once it's frozen;
> - **holds mould dormant** — freezing does *not* kill mould spores, but it stops them growing while you
>   plan (and mould can bloom within 48 hours of a soaking);
> - **buys indefinite time** to assess value, sort replace-vs-restore, arrange a drying vendor and prep a
>   rehabilitation space.
>
> Frozen material can wait weeks or months in stable condition. The one rule: **freeze fast** — ideally
> within **6–8 hours** of the items getting wet.

### 3c. New section — "How vacuum freeze-drying actually works"
> Once material is frozen, vacuum freeze-drying finishes the job:
> 1. The frozen books/records go into a **vacuum chamber.**
> 2. A vacuum is pulled and **controlled heat** is added — but the material is kept **below 0 °C (32 °F)**,
>    so it never thaws.
> 3. Under low pressure, the ice **sublimates** — goes straight to vapour — and is drawn off to a cold
>    condensing coil.
> 4. The book dries in the shape it was frozen in, so **press it flat before freezing** and it comes out
>    flat.
>
> Freeze quickly and the payoff is dramatic: material frozen soon after wetting needs only **8% or less**
> extra shelf space when dry, where air-dried volumes swell and distort and need far more. Blast-freezing
> (rapidly below **−18 °C**) forms smaller ice crystals and does even less damage.

### 3d. New section — "The 6-hour rule for glossy paper"
> Coated / glossy paper (magazines, art books, glossy reports) is the emergency inside the emergency. Wet
> coated pages will **"block"** — fuse together into a solid brick — and once blocked they're usually
> unrecoverable. The rule: get coated stock **frozen within about six hours** of wetting, and **never
> air-dry it** — it must be **vacuum freeze-dried.** If in doubt about a mixed load, freeze first and sort
> the drying method later; freezing itself buys you the decision time.

### 3e. New section — "Where it goes wrong: over-drying and the terminology trap"
> Two traps catch people:
> - **Over-drying / embrittlement.** A vacuum chamber can pull *too much* moisture out, leaving paper
>   brittle — and it's worst for **leather, vellum and parchment**, which naturally hold about twice the
>   moisture of paper. Good vendors build in a **reacclimation / re-humidification** step so materials
>   return to their natural moisture content and flexibility.
> - **The word "freeze-drying" is used loosely.** *True* vacuum **freeze-drying** keeps everything frozen
>   and sublimates — safe. *Vacuum **thermal** drying* freezes and thaws through the liquid state — it
>   causes heat damage, distortion and mould risk and is **not recommended** for any collection material.
>   Before you hire a vendor, confirm which process they actually run.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> The trick that saves a soaked rare book is the same physics that makes instant coffee: **sublimation.**
> Keep the water frozen and pull a vacuum, and the ice turns straight to vapour without ever melting — so
> the ink never gets a chance to run and the pages never cockle. A book can come out of a freeze-drying
> chamber flatter and cleaner than it went in, because the one thing that does the damage — liquid water —
> was skipped entirely.
