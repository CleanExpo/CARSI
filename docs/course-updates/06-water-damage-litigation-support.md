## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "The standard moved: IICRC S500 2026"
> If your course notes still cite the 2021 S500, know that the **2026 revision is now the published
> standard** — and standard of care is judged against the current version. The change that matters most
> for litigation support: documentation has been promoted from *supporting* material to the *central*
> requirement. A moisture map, atmospheric readings and material-moisture readings are no longer
> "nice-to-have evidence" — under the 2026 language they **are** the evidence that the work met the
> standard. The revision also tightens the Category/Class definitions (when a job is Cat 3, when it's
> Class 4) and sharpens the line where an S500 drying job becomes an S520 remediation job. Loosely
> documented determinations are now much easier to challenge in a dispute.

### 3b. New section — "Why cases are won or lost at the first inspection"
> In the standard, **"should" means required** — it's part of the accepted standard of care. The initial
> inspection has to document the source and time of the intrusion, pre-existing damage, and moisture
> migration *into* the materials — meaning it must be **intrusive**, not a surface walk-through, and it
> must happen **immediately**. This is not box-ticking: you cannot reliably establish months later
> whether damage is new (typically covered) or long-term/pre-existing (often excluded). The contractor
> who does an intrusive, science-based, standard-compliant inspection on day one holds the advantage in
> any later dispute — even when the results aren't perfectly clear-cut.

### 3c. New section — "A real case: how an expert got thrown out (US, 2025)"
> *(US illustration — the law is US, but the lesson is universal.)* In a 2025 US federal appeal, a
> building owner's water-damage claim collapsed because their causation expert was **excluded** by the
> court. Why? His investigation was thin: he never inspected the interior damage, never cut or tested the
> roof membrane, leaned on "eliminating other causes," and hadn't reviewed a supplemental report that
> identified a failed roofing seam. With his opinion excluded, the owner had no admissible evidence of a
> covered loss and lost outright. The takeaway for anyone supporting a claim: **an opinion is only as
> strong as the investigation under it.** Inspect thoroughly, test rather than assume, and address every
> competing explanation in your documentation — not after you're challenged, but before.

### 3d. "Interesting fact" hook
> A water-damage claim worth six figures was lost not because the damage wasn't real, but because the
> expert didn't cut a single sample of the roof he blamed. The evidence that would have won the case was
> a two-hour job on day one — and it was never collected.
