## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Universal precautions: treat everything as infectious"
> The foundation of biohazard work is a single discipline called **universal precautions**: because you
> cannot tell by looking whether a body fluid carries a bloodborne pathogen, **you treat every body fluid
> as if it does.** No guessing, no "it's probably fine" — same PPE, same handling, same disposal, every
> time. That's not caution for its own sake; it's the only defensible standard when the risk is invisible.
> It drives everything downstream: employer-funded PPE that actually blocks contact (gloves, moisture-
> resistant coveralls, respiratory and eye protection), and hard rules on sharps — contaminated needles are
> **never** recapped or bent, and contaminated broken glass is lifted with a brush and pan or tongs, never
> by hand.

### 3b. New section — "Physical removal is the primary remediation"
> The most misunderstood point in biohazard work: **disinfectant is not the remediation.** The standard is
> explicit that the *physical removal and proper disposal* of blood and OPIM is the primary means of
> remediation — disinfection comes **after**, to treat what remains once the material itself is gone. You
> cannot spray your way clean over contaminated material; it has to be physically removed and disposed of as
> **regulated waste** — contained in durable, leakproof, labelled containers and destroyed by an approved
> route (autoclave or incineration). Only then does a government-registered antimicrobial do its job.

### 3c. New section — "The eight principles (the spine of every job)"
> Trauma and crime scene cleanup runs on eight principles, in order:
> 1. **Safety & health** — identify and control every biohazard risk before work begins.
> 2. **Documentation** — written and photographic records throughout (for insurance, compliance, transparency).
> 3. **Inspection & risk assessment** — determine the extent of contamination and scope the work.
> 4. **Containment & engineering controls** — barriers, airflow and filtration to stop cross-contamination
>    into clean areas.
> 5. **Physical removal** — the primary remediation (above).
> 6. **Cleaning** — of affected structure and contents.
> 7. **Disinfection** — a government-registered antimicrobial applied after removal and cleaning.
> 8. **Confirmation** — verify the work is done.
>
> A scene is only worked **after law enforcement or the regulator has released it** — the standard assumes
> that release as its starting point.

### 3d. New section — "Who verifies it's clean"
> "Clean" is confirmed at two levels. A **PRE** — post-remediation evaluation — is the technician's own
> assessment that cleaning and antimicrobial application worked. Where law or contract requires it, a
> **PRV** — post-remediation verification — is performed by an independent **IEP** (Indoor Environmental
> Professional), a qualified third party who verifies the site is free of physical and microbial
> contamination. The two-tier check keeps the contractor honest and gives the client (and insurer) an
> independent sign-off on a job where "looks clean" is nowhere near good enough.

### 3e. Jurisdiction box (Australia)
> The regulatory detail above (OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens, HAZWOPER, CDC guidance) is **United States** law,
> used here to illustrate the safety framework — it is **not** Australian law. In Australia, biohazard and
> trauma cleaning is governed by **Safe Work Australia** model WHS laws and the relevant **state/territory
> WHS regulator**, with AS/NZS standards for PPE and waste handling and state clinical/biohazard-waste
> rules for disposal. The *principles* — universal precautions, physical removal first, PPE, regulated-waste
> disposal, independent verification — are universal; the citations and agencies differ. Always work to the
> current AU regulation for your jurisdiction.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> In biohazard work you never diagnose the fluid — you assume the worst. It's called **universal
> precautions**: since no one can look at a spill and know whether it carries a pathogen, the rule is to
> treat *every* body fluid as infectious. And the cleanup itself follows a counter-intuitive order — the
> disinfectant isn't the fix. **Physical removal is**; you can't sanitise your way over material that has to
> be taken out and destroyed as regulated waste.
