## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "There's finally a standard: ANSI/IICRC S700 (2025)"
> For years, fire and smoke restoration had no consensus standard the way water damage had S500. That
> changed in January 2025: **ANSI/IICRC S700** is the first American National Standard for professional
> fire and smoke damage restoration — more than a decade in the making. It's now the reference for what
> "done" means, and it moves the bar in one important way: **completion is defined by client-verified
> undetectability of residues and odours**, not by a surface passing a visual look. A job that looks
> clean but still smells is, by the standard, not finished. S700 also makes **HVAC inspection mandatory
> on every fire loss** — because smoke travels through ductwork into rooms that never saw a flame.

### 3b. New section — "The fire isn't the end of the damage"
> The most expensive misunderstanding in fire restoration is thinking the damage stops when the fire is
> out. It doesn't. Smoke residues and corrosive off-gases keep etching and penetrating surfaces for
> **24–72 hours** after suppression. Acidic synthetic soot can pit glass and corrode chrome and copper
> within a day. That's why the scope you can achieve on day one is a completely different scope from day
> three — speed of response is part of the restoration quality, not just customer service.

### 3c. New section — "Four soot types, four chemistries (get this wrong and you make it worse)"
> Treating all soot as one substance is the core beginner mistake. What burned decides the chemistry:
> - **Dry soot** (wood, paper, natural fibres): powdery, loosely stuck. **HEPA-vacuum and dry-sponge it
>   FIRST.** Wet cleaning applied first smears it deep into the pores and makes the job harder.
> - **Wet synthetic soot** (plastics, foam): thick, sticky, **highly acidic (pH ~3–4)** — etches glass
>   in 24–48 hours. Needs strong alkaline chemistry (pH 11–13); dry methods just smear it.
> - **Protein residue** (kitchen fires): near-invisible amber film, severe penetrating odour, bonds
>   tightly — needs **enzyme** chemistry. It's the single most common source of "you didn't finish"
>   odour callbacks, because techs miss it on the visual pass.
> - **Fuel-oil / puffback** (furnace): oily black film that migrates through the HVAC and soaks into porous surfaces.
>
> The rule S700 states plainly: *single procedures rarely remove all residue* — combined methods
> (dry, then wet, then odour treatment) are the standard, not over-servicing.

### 3d. New section — "Why the odour comes back"
> Persistent odour after a "finished" job is almost always one of three things: the residue type was
> misidentified (protein treated like ordinary soot), the HVAC was never remediated (the ducts keep
> re-seeding odour into clean rooms), or the VOCs are still off-gassing from deep inside drywall and
> framing where cleaning only reached the surface. And the deodorising tool has to match the occupancy:
> **thermal fogging and ozone need the building fully evacuated** (ozone is toxic and wrecks rubber,
> artwork and electronics), while **hydroxyl generation is the only acceptable choice when people or pets
> are present.**

### 3e. Bushfire box (Australia)
> For bushfire/wildfire smoke (per the 2025 IICRC/RIA/CIRI wildfire guide), a structure can be impacted
> without ever catching fire: within roughly **1–10 km of the fire** ("near-field zone"), plume pressure
> can force soot, char, ash and gaseous byproducts into the building envelope. Look for the tell-tales —
> **filtration marks** under doors, **soot-coated spider webs** in ceiling corners, and **"ghosting"**
> (dark outlines of the studs where charged soot particles were drawn to the framing). Combustion
> byproducts of concern include VOCs and heavier SVOCs (PAHs, dioxins) that carry the lingering odour.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> A house a kilometre from a bushfire can be seriously smoke-damaged without a single flame touching it —
> and one of the clearest signs is the spider webs: soot sticks to them so well that restorers read the
> blackened webs in the ceiling corners as evidence the smoke got in.
