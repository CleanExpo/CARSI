## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "The mask is the last line, not the first"
> It's tempting to treat a respirator as the answer to a hazardous atmosphere. The safety framework says the
> opposite: PPE sits **dead last** in the hierarchy of controls — after elimination, substitution,
> engineering controls (containment, ventilation, HEPA filtration) and administrative controls. Why last?
> Because PPE protects only the one person wearing it, and only for as long as it's worn correctly. So the
> first question on a hazardous job isn't "which respirator?" — it's "can I *engineer* the hazard down
> first?" The respirator is what covers the residual risk, not the whole plan. And the moment a respirator
> is required, so is a **written respiratory protection program**: hazard assessment, medical clearance, fit
> testing and training. It's not optional paperwork — it's what makes the respirator actually protective.

### 3b. New section — "Reading a respirator: N/R/P and 95/99/100"
> The code on a filter tells you exactly what it does:
> - **The letter is about oil:** **N** = *not* oil-resistant, **R** = oil-*resistant*, **P** = oil-*proof*.
> - **The number is efficiency:** **95**, **99**, or **100** percent of the test particles captured. A
>   **P100** (magenta filter) captures 99.97% — HEPA-equivalent.
>
> So an "N95" is a not-oil-resistant, 95%-efficient particulate filter — fine for nuisance dust, not for oily
> smoke aerosols. When there are oils or solvents in the air (fire/smoke, some chemicals), you need an **R**
> or **P** filter, and often an **organic-vapour (OV) cartridge** on top of the particulate filter.

### 3c. New section — "APF: how much protection you're actually getting"
> Every class of respirator has an **Assigned Protection Factor (APF)** — the multiple by which it lowers
> your exposure when used properly:
> - **N95 / half-mask: APF 10** (reduces exposure ~10×)
> - **Full-face elastomeric: APF 50**
> - **Tight-fitting PAPR / full-face powered: APF up to 1,000** (loose-fitting hood ~25)
> - **Supplied-air / SCBA: up to 10,000**
>
> The APF only holds if the respirator is correctly selected, fitted and used inside a real program — the
> number on the box is a ceiling, not a guarantee.

### 3d. New section — "Match the respirator to the job"
> The right respirator scales with the hazard:
> - **Mould under ~100 ft²:** a half- or full-face **N/R/P-95** at minimum. (Disposable N95s suit only the
>   smallest jobs — their fit degrades over a long shift.)
> - **Mould over ~100 ft², heavy/blanket growth, or heavy dust:** step up to **N/R/P-100**.
> - **Fire and smoke:** commonly a **half-face P100 with an organic-vapour cartridge** (oils + odour compounds).
> - **Mould, trauma and sewage remediation:** a **full-face respirator** is the norm — it protects the eyes
>   as well and seals better.
> - **High-contamination / large jobs:** a **PAPR** (powered air) for higher protection and comfort over
>   long durations.

### 3e. New section — "Fit test, seal check, and the warning signs"
> A respirator only delivers its APF if it seals to *your* face, so two checks matter:
> - **Fit test** — a formal test (qualitative by taste/smell, or quantitative by instrument) done **before
>   first use, every year, and any time** the respirator model changes or your face does (significant weight
>   change, dental work, scarring). Half-masks must reach a fit factor of **100**, full-face **500**.
> - **User seal check** — a quick positive/negative-pressure check you do **every single time you put it
>   on**, not just at the start of the shift.
>
> Learn the warning signs the seal has gone: **you can smell the contaminant**, your goggles fog, or you
> hear air whistling around the nosepiece (often a missing gasket). One caution that saves lives — **no
> smell does not mean you're safe.** Plenty of the worst hazards are odourless, so the nose is a failure
> alarm, never a safety clearance.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> The respirator is the *last* thing the safety hierarchy reaches for, not the first — because it protects
> only the person wearing it, and only if it seals. And here's the counter-intuitive part techs get wrong:
> smelling the contaminant through your mask means the seal has failed — but *not* smelling anything proves
> nothing, because many of the most dangerous things in the air have no smell at all. The mask that feels
> fine can still be failing.
