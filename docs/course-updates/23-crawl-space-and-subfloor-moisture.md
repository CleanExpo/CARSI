## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "You breathe your crawl space"
> A crawl space isn't a sealed-off void under the house — it's connected to the air you breathe. Through the
> **stack effect** (warm air rising and drawing replacement air up from below), **40–50% of the air on a
> home's first floor is pulled up from the crawl space.** So whatever is in that crawl space — mould spores,
> allergens, moisture, musty odour — is being drawn into the living space every day. That's why crawl-space
> moisture is never *only* a structural problem; it's an indoor-air-quality problem for the people upstairs.

### 3b. New section — "Four ways water gets in"
> Moisture reaches a crawl space by four distinct routes, and each needs a different fix:
> - **Bulk water** — rain and groundwater through foundation cracks or poor grading. Highest volume. Fix
>   with drainage, grading and waterproofing.
> - **Capillary action** — water wicking up through porous concrete and masonry. Fix with a capillary
>   break / vapour barrier.
> - **Air-transported moisture** — humid air carried through floor penetrations by the stack effect. This is
>   the dominant mechanism in most climates — **50–100× more moisture than diffusion** — which is why *air
>   sealing beats adding vents.* (A single one-inch hole in the subfloor moves more moisture per day than
>   30 sq ft of bare concrete does by diffusion.)
> - **Vapour diffusion** — molecules passing straight through solid material, driven by vapour pressure.
>   Slowest but constant: exposed soil gives off roughly **10–15 gallons a day per 1,000 sq ft.** Fix with a
>   ground vapour barrier.

### 3c. New section — "Why venting a humid crawl space makes it wetter"
> For decades codes required crawl-space vents, on the intuition that outside air carries moisture away.
> Building science overturned that — and the mechanism is worth understanding. In a humid climate, the warm
> outdoor air you're inviting in holds a *lot* of water. When it enters the crawl space and hits the cooler
> surfaces (subfloor, ducts, foundation walls), it cools; cool air holds less moisture, so its relative
> humidity climbs — often to **95–100%** — and it **condenses** on every surface below the dew point.
> Venting doesn't dry the crawl space; in summer it actively *wets* it. The more vents, the more humid air
> admitted, and often the higher the wood moisture. (This is a humid-climate effect — in genuinely dry
> climates, drier outdoor air can help.)

### 3d. New section — "Sealed vs vented — what the research shows"
> The field data is decisive for humid climates. Advanced Energy's North Carolina study measured sealed,
> conditioned crawl spaces at about **52% RH** against roughly **77%** in vented ones — the difference
> between a mould-free environment and near-guaranteed colonisation, since mould starts within 24–48 hours
> above 60% RH. Wood moisture content followed: **10–14%** (safe) in sealed spaces versus **19%+** in
> vented, where **decay fungi activate** and quietly consume structural timber until a floor feels soft.
> Sealed also cut heating/cooling loads. In humid climates, sealed and conditioned wins on every measured
> axis; in dry climates, both approaches can work.

### 3e. New section — "Never just close the vents"
> The most damaging half-measure is blocking the foundation vents and stopping there. Seal the vents without
> a ground vapour barrier and humidity control, and you've **trapped the soil moisture with no way out** —
> often making things worse than the leaky vented space you started with. Encapsulation is a *system*, in order:
> 1. **Control the bulk water first** — grade soil away from the foundation, clean and extend gutters/
>    downspouts, add a sump if groundwater rises. Drainage is the first line of defence.
> 2. **Cap the ground** with a continuous **Class I vapour retarder (≤0.1 perm)**, seams overlapped and
>    sealed, run up the wall.
> 3. **Seal** the vents and rim joists and insulate the perimeter.
> 4. **Condition** the space — a trickle of HVAC air, code-sized mechanical exhaust, or a crawl-space
>    dehumidifier holding **RH below ~50%.**
>
> Skip a step and the system leaks; do all four and subfloor mould stops recurring.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> Opening the vents to "air out" a damp crawl space usually makes it **wetter.** In a humid summer, the warm
> outdoor air you let in cools against the cool subfloor and ducts and dumps its moisture as condensation —
> the exact opposite of the intent. For decades the building codes had it backwards. And because the stack
> effect draws up to **half of your ground-floor air** straight from that crawl space, whatever is growing
> down there, you're breathing upstairs.
