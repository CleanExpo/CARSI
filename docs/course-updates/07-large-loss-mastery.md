## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "What actually makes a loss 'large'"
> "Large loss" isn't a feeling about a big job — it's a threshold that flips how the whole project runs.
> The common markers: damage over about US$100,000 (adjust to AUD for an Australian loss), **multiple
> floors or tenant spaces affected, or more than ten days of active mitigation**. Cross any one of those
> and the job changes shape: you now need a dedicated project manager rather than a lead technician,
> formal daily reporting to the carrier's large-loss specialist, a written timeline with milestones, and
> a pre-work meeting with the owner, carrier and tenants before a single air mover goes down.

### 3b. New section — "The stakeholder map (and why it's the real job)"
> A residential job has one owner and one adjuster. A commercial large loss has a crowd: the building
> owner, the building manager (whose authorisation actually drives daily decisions), the commercial
> carrier and its large-loss adjuster, **every tenant**, **every tenant's own insurer**, and your team —
> often with a **TPA** sitting between you and the carrier, approving every scope change through its
> system. Manage that on informal phone calls and you manufacture conflicting authorisations and dispute
> surface. The professionals run a written protocol: one daily project log to everyone, a single point of
> contact per organisation, and **written change orders for every scope change — no verbal approvals.**

### 3c. New section — "Business interruption is part of your job"
> On a commercial loss, the money isn't only in the building — it's in the **business income the tenants
> lose while they can't operate.** Here's the part restorers miss: **your milestone timeline is the
> primary document that establishes the business-interruption period.** When each area returns to use,
> logged and dated, defines what the tenant can claim. And it cuts both ways — delays *you* cause
> (equipment shortfalls, scheduling failures) may not extend the BI clock, while delays from permits or
> abatement typically do. The restorer who can sequence work to reopen the highest-revenue areas first,
> and prove the timeline, is worth far more to the client than one who only reports moisture readings.

### 3d. Operating-discipline box
> - **Cross the threshold, change the structure.** PM not lead-tech; daily reporting; written milestones.
> - **Every scope change is a written change order** through the TPA — never a verbal yes.
> - **Document deeper:** daily per-area photo logs + timestamped moisture readings to dry standard.
> - **Don't forget the HVAC scope** — mandatory and separately lined in healthcare/food/pharma.
> - **Own the handoff.** A 10–20 page mitigation prep standard, built from your last 90 days of failures
>   and revised quarterly, is where large-loss margin is protected.

### 3e. "Interesting fact" hook
> On a large commercial loss, the single most valuable document you produce isn't the moisture log — it's
> the timeline. It's the piece of paper that decides how much business-interruption money a dozen tenants
> can claim, and it has your name on it.
