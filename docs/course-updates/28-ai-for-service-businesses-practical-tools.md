## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Where AI actually helps a service business"
> Forget the hype for a second. In Australia, AI has already crossed into the mainstream for small business —
> about **42% of SMEs use it today** (NAB), and by some measures 59% do once you count the AI baked into
> software you already run. The single most common place owners use it isn't robots or self-driving trucks —
> it's **marketing and sales** (51% of AU SME users). This course is about the boring, profitable end of AI: the
> quote you send faster, the follow-up email that actually goes out, the review reply you didn't have time to
> write. If it doesn't save you time or win you a job, we don't cover it.

### 3b. New section — "The four jobs to start with"
> The owners getting value don't "adopt AI" — they point it at four specific jobs where the use-case is obvious
> and the risk is low:
> 1. **Quoting** — draft the quote body and scope wording from a few notes (54% of trade AI users start here).
> 2. **Invoicing & admin** — chase-ups, payment reminders, tidy descriptions (52%).
> 3. **Business writing** — emails, proposals, job descriptions, website copy (51%; content generation is the
>    #1 use in Australia at 54%).
> 4. **Making sense of your numbers** — data analytics on jobs, channels and costs (the other AU #1 at 54%).
>
> Notice what's *not* on the list: anything that touches a customer without you reading it first. Start narrow,
> win time back, then widen.

### 3c. New section — "Closing the relevance gap"
> Here's the most important statistic in Australian small business AI: **54% of owners who haven't adopted it
> say 'AI isn't relevant to my business.'** That's not a rejection — it's a missing example. Nobody's shown them
> what it looks like for a water-damage crew or a carpet-cleaning outfit. And the trades are exactly where
> adoption lags: **under 30% in Construction.** Read that two ways. One: you're not behind your customers'
> expectations yet. Two: the competitor down the road hasn't worked it out either — so the owner who does gets a
> genuine edge before the rest catch up. This course exists to be the relatable example.

### 3d. New section — "Keep a human on the wire"
> The number-one reason Australian owners *don't* use AI is **trust** — around 65% either distrust AI decisions
> or want to keep humans in control. That instinct is correct, and it's also the unlock. The rule that makes AI
> safe to use in a service business is simple: **AI drafts, a human sends.** About half of AU AI users already
> check outputs before they reach a customer — make that your non-negotiable. Never let AI quote a price,
> promise a timeline, or hit "send" on its own. Used that way, AI is a fast junior who never gets tired and
> always needs checking — which is exactly what a busy owner needs.

### 3e. New section — "Your first 30-day AI plan"
> - **Week 1:** pick ONE job from the four (usually quoting or email). Draft with AI, edit, send. Notice the time.
> - **Week 2:** build a reusable prompt for that job (your services, your tone, your suburb). Reuse it daily.
> - **Week 3:** add a second job. Write one house rule: *nothing customer-facing goes out unread.*
> - **Week 4:** tally the hours saved and jobs touched. Keep what earned its place; drop what didn't.
>
> That's it. No platform migration, no big spend — a fortnight of small wins beats a year of "we should look
> into AI."

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> The thing holding most Australian tradies and service businesses back from AI isn't cost or complexity — it's
> that **54% of owners simply say "it's not relevant to my business."** But that's not a considered no; it's the
> absence of a single relatable example. The moment an owner sees AI write *their* quote, in *their* voice, for
> *their* suburb, the objection evaporates. Meanwhile fewer than a third of construction-adjacent businesses have
> started — so the relevance gap is also the widest open door in small business right now.

## Block 5 · Considered & rejected
1. **Naming specific AI products/tools** — rejected: tool names date fast and vary by market; the course teaches
   the *four jobs* and the *human-on-the-wire* rule, which survive any tool churn. A dated "current tools" annex
   can be maintained separately.
2. **A "future of AI / AGI" module** — rejected as off-purpose; owners need this-quarter wins, not speculation.
3. **US home-services dollar figures as headline** — kept only as illustrative mechanism; AU data (NAB, National
   AI Centre) leads every claim per the Australian-produced mandate.

## Conversion / learning hypothesis (M-2)
- **Metric:** completion + "started using AI for at least one job within 30 days" self-report; quiz on the four
  jobs + the human-on-the-wire rule.
- **Target:** ≥40% of finishers report a live AI use within 30 days.
- **Kill threshold:** revise if completion <50% or the 30-day-use rate <25%.
- **Next variant:** if flat, lead with 3c (relevance gap) as the course spine and add a restoration-specific worked example.
