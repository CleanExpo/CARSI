## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Why the fastest quote wins"
> Most owners think they lose jobs on price. They lose them on **speed.** The data is blunt: **78% of buyers
> hire whoever responds first**, and the average business takes nearly **two days** to reply. In the gap between
> a customer's enquiry and your call-back, they've rung two competitors and booked one. You didn't lose on
> quality or price — you lost because someone else answered first. Speed-to-lead is the cheapest competitive
> advantage in a service business, and almost nobody uses it.

### 3b. New section — "The 5-minute rule"
> Here's the number worth putting on the wall: respond within **5 minutes** and you're **21 times more likely**
> to qualify that lead than if you wait 30. Between 5 and 10 minutes, your odds fall by roughly ten times. In
> Australia the tradie version is the same — a lead not contacted within about **30 minutes** has usually
> already called someone else. This isn't about working harder; it's about the first five minutes mattering more
> than the next five hours. Build your day so a new enquiry gets *some* human-quality response inside that window.

### 3c. New section — "The missed-call text-back"
> You can't answer every call — you're on a job, up a ladder, under a house. So the highest-ROI automation in a
> service business isn't fancy: it's an **automatic SMS to every missed call.** Something like: *"Hi, it's
> [Name] from [Business] — sorry I missed your call. I'll ring you back within the hour. Is this the best number
> to reach you?"* That one message keeps you in the race while you finish the job, and it recovers a real share
> of leads that would otherwise ring the next name on Google. It costs cents and it runs itself.

### 3d. New section — "Follow-up is a system, not a memory"
> One call is not follow-up. The difference is stark: structured multi-touch follow-up pulls roughly **90%
> response versus 9%** for one-and-done, and businesses with a proper sequence close **15–25% more** of their
> quotes. "I'll give them a buzz if I remember" is how quotes die. A simple, written sequence — call, then SMS
> the quote, then a check-in in two days, then a last nudge in a week — converts the enquiries you already paid
> to get. The leads are the expensive part; the follow-up is nearly free and it's where the money is left.

### 3e. New section — "Know your numbers by channel"
> AU trade lead costs jumped **~21%** in a year while conversion **fell ~12%** — the squeeze is real, so guessing
> is expensive. Cost-per-lead alone lies: a **$60** Facebook lead that takes four calls and converts 12% is
> dearer, in real terms, than a **$120** Google Local Services lead that converts 40% on one call. Track, per
> channel, **cost per lead** and **cost per booked job** — kill any channel that can't prove a booked job within
> 90 days. Referrals convert at **65%+**, so a referral habit beats a bigger ad budget almost every time.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> The most profitable thing a service business can do costs almost nothing: **answer faster.** Reply to an
> enquiry within five minutes and you're **21 times more likely** to qualify it than the business that waits
> thirty — and since **78% of buyers hire whoever responds first**, speed quietly decides most jobs before price
> is ever discussed. The kicker for Australian owners: the average first response is measured in *days*, so a
> single automated missed-call text can put you ahead of nearly every competitor in your suburb.
