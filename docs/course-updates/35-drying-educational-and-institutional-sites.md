## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Drying around children changes the plan"
> A flooded classroom isn't a flooded office. The occupants are children — a sensitive population whose asthma
> and allergies are set off by damp and mould, and whose learning suffers in a musty room — and the building
> runs on a calendar that can't simply pause. So drying an educational site is a balancing act the technical
> textbook doesn't cover: get the moisture out fast enough to beat mould, without disrupting classes, exposing
> kids to spores or dust, or tripping over the regulated materials hiding in an older building. The
> psychrometrics are ordinary; the constraints are not.

### 3b. New section — "The calendar is your biggest tool"
> On a school job, *when* you work matters as much as *how*. The playbook:
> - **Major work → school breaks.** Summer, term breaks and long weekends are when big dry-outs and rebuilds
>   belong, so students return to a finished space.
> - **In-term emergencies → after hours + phased zones.** Contain the affected area, run louder tasks after the
>   bell, and keep unaffected wings operational so teaching continues.
> - **Communicate constantly** with facilities and the principal/estates team — logistics, timelines, which
>   rooms come back when. On big events, relocatable ("demountable") classrooms bridge the gap.
> The 24–48-hour drying window doesn't pause for the timetable, so speed *and* scheduling both have to work.

### 3c. New section — "Kids are the sensitive population"
> Design every decision around who's breathing the air. Children are more vulnerable to mould-driven asthma and
> allergy flare-ups than adults, so the acceptable margin is smaller. Hold indoor humidity around **30–60%**, hit
> the **24–48-hour** window to stop mould before it starts, and remember the **HVAC is a pathway** — run it
> through a wet zone and you seed spores into classrooms across the school. If the moisture source isn't
> permanently fixed, the mould (and the closures) come back. Fixing the leak is part of the dry-out, not a later
> job for someone else.

### 3d. New section — "Older buildings: asbestos and lead"
> Australia built a lot of schools in the asbestos era, and older institutional buildings routinely hide
> **asbestos** (in flooring, wall sheeting, insulation) and **lead paint.** During a water loss, the tempting
> move — pull up the wet vinyl, cut out the soaked wall sheet — is exactly what disturbs those materials. That's
> a **licensed-removal** trigger, not a drying task: identify suspect materials, stop, and bring in licensed
> asbestos removal before demolition-style strip-out. In Australia this runs under **Safe Work Australia** and
> the **Asbestos Safety and Eradication Agency** framework, with state WHS regulators. When in doubt, treat it as
> asbestos until it's cleared.

### 3e. New section — "Working With Children and safeguarding"
> Working on a school site adds duties that have nothing to do with drying. Technicians need the appropriate
> **Working With Children Check** and must follow the site's **safeguarding** protocols — signing in, staying in
> approved areas, and never being alone with students. Access is conditional and supervised. It's not
> bureaucracy to work around; it's the baseline that lets you be on site at all, and getting it wrong ends the
> job (and the relationship) faster than any technical error.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> On a school water loss, the clock and the calendar pull in opposite directions — and both win. Mould can take
> hold in **24–48 hours**, so the drying can't wait; yet the *disruptive* work is deliberately pushed to nights,
> weekends and school holidays so children never share the air with the dust, spores or noise. The best school
> restorers are judged less on their drying than on a quieter metric: the students came back to a finished,
> tested, safe room — and never knew the crew had been there. And the completion test isn't "it looks dry", it's
> an **independent air-quality clearance** before the space is handed back.
