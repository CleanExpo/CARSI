## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "The three Conditions (know which one you're in)"
> Professional mould work is scoped against three defined **Conditions** (from the S520 standard):
> - **Condition 1 — normal fungal ecology.** Settled spores at levels you'd expect indoors. No remediation.
> - **Condition 2 — settled spores.** Spores or fragments that drifted in from a Condition 3 area, maybe
>   with trace growth. Cleaning and source correction.
> - **Condition 3 — actual growth.** Live or dormant, visible or hidden mould colonising the material. Full
>   remediation.
>
> Every job is really one question: *which Condition is each area in, and how do I get all of them back to
> Condition 1?* Condition 1 is the finish line — not "no visible mould," but a normal fungal ecology
> comparable to the outdoor and unaffected baseline.

### 3b. New section — "Removal is the remediation — not killing"
> The single most important principle in the standard: **physically removing the mould is the primary means
> of remediation.** Trying to kill, encapsulate or inhibit it *instead of* removing the source is, in the
> standard's words, generally not adequate. Here's why killing isn't enough: **dead mould is still
> allergenic, and some dead moulds are still toxic.** Spraying a biocide over a colony changes its status
> from "alive" to "dead" but leaves the allergens and mycotoxins in place — and leaves the contaminated
> material in the building. Antimicrobials have a role, but only *after* and *with* cleaning, never as the
> main event.

### 3c. New section — "Why bleach is the wrong tool"
> Bleach is the instinctive choice and the classic mistake. Two reasons:
> 1. **It can't reach the roots.** Mould in drywall or wood sends microscopic filaments (hyphae) millimetres
>    into the material. Bleach sits on the surface; it can't penetrate to where the colony actually lives.
> 2. **It delivers water to the mould.** Household bleach is roughly **90% water.** On porous material the
>    chlorine flashes off while the water soaks in — feeding the very colony you're trying to kill.
>
> The EPA doesn't recommend bleach for routine mould cleanup (detergent and water on hard surfaces is its
> first-line advice), and the S520 standard doesn't accept it as a substitute for physical removal on
> porous materials. On **non-porous** surfaces bleach can work, but 3% hydrogen peroxide or detergent does
> the same job with fewer fumes. On **porous** materials, nothing sprayed on top works — remove and replace.

### 3d. New section — "The fixed cleaning sequence"
> On the salvageable surfaces that stay, the cleaning order is fixed — and reversing it is a red flag:
> 1. **HEPA vacuum** every surface (pipes, wire runs, duct exteriors, frames) to capture loose spores.
> 2. **Damp-wipe with a detergent solution** to lift residual contamination and break the bond of sticky
>    mycotoxins.
> 3. **Second HEPA pass** to capture what the wipe released.
> 4. **Only then**, an EPA-registered antimicrobial — *if* it's warranted.
>
> Cleaning always precedes any chemical. On semi-porous framing, wire-brushing, sanding or media-blasting
> (soda or dry-ice) may come first to lift embedded growth. If a contractor's plan leads with fogging or
> antimicrobial spray as the *removal* method, that's the tell they're cutting the corner that brings the
> mould back. And none of it holds unless the **moisture source is fixed first** — "the key to mould control
> is moisture control."

### 3e. New section — "Who signs it off: PRE vs PRV"
> "Done" is confirmed at two levels. The remediator performs a **PRE** — post-remediation evaluation —
> checking the area is visibly clean, dust-free and free of microbial odour, so it's *probably* back to
> Condition 1. Then, where required, an **independent IEP** performs a **PRV** — post-remediation
> verification — with visual inspection, moisture readings and air/surface sampling compared to an outdoor
> baseline, to *verify* Condition 1. The IEP must be independent of the remediator (or disclose in writing
> that they aren't). One caution: **air sampling tests the air, not the surfaces** — on previously affected
> framing, include surface (tape/swab) sampling, or a clean air result can mask surface contamination.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> Reaching for bleach on mouldy drywall is worse than doing nothing — because household bleach is about 90%
> water. The chlorine bleaches the surface you can see while the water soaks straight into the board and
> **feeds the roots of the colony you can't.** It's why both the EPA and the professional S520 standard say
> the same thing: you don't kill your way out of mould in porous material — you cut it out.
