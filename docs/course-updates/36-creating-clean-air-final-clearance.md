## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Clearance is a decision, not a formality"
> Handover is the moment the job is judged. Everything before it — extraction, drying, containment, cleaning —
> exists to reach one defensible decision: *this space is safe to give back.* Clearance isn't a signature at the
> end of a form; it's a verification step with a defined pass mark. Get it right and the owner reoccupies with
> confidence and the job closes cleanly. Get it wrong — sign off a room that still has a hidden moisture source
> or elevated indoor spores — and the mould comes back, the complaint lands, and the "finished" job reopens as a
> dispute. Treat clearance as the most important inspection of the project, because it is.

### 3b. New section — "Clean means *back to normal*, not sterile"
> The target has a name: **Condition 1** — a normal fungal ecology, the kind of low, ordinary spore background
> you'd find in a similar building that never flooded. It is *not* a sterile, spore-free room. You cannot make
> indoor air spore-free, and chasing that is the wrong goal that leads people to spray coatings over problems
> instead of removing them. The correct end-state is "indistinguishable from a normal, unaffected indoor
> environment," achieved by **physically removing** the contamination and fixing the moisture — not by killing,
> encapsulating or masking it. If the only thing standing between the mould and the room is a coat of paint,
> it hasn't been remediated.

### 3c. New section — "The eyes-and-nose test does most of the work"
> Before anyone reaches for a sampling pump, the single most important part of clearance is a **detailed visual
> and olfactory inspection.** The checklist is concrete: the water source is **corrected**; containment and air
> filtration (AFDs) were properly used; there is **no visible mould**; there is **no visible dust** — wipe a
> white or black towel across a surface and it comes back clean; and there is **no musty/mouldy odour.** Most
> failed clearances fail here, on things you can see and smell, long before a lab result matters. If it looks
> clean, smells clean, and the leak is fixed, you're most of the way to Condition 1.

### 3d. New section — "Read the air against the outdoors"
> When air sampling *is* used, the rookie mistake is reading it as a single pass/fail number. It isn't. Clearance
> air sampling is **qualitative and comparative**: you take an **indoor** spore-trap sample and an **outdoor
> reference** sample, and you ask whether the indoor *types and proportions* of fungi look like the outdoor
> baseline. Here's the counter-intuitive part — the indoor count can come back *higher* than it was before
> remediation and still **pass**, if the outdoor level is high that day and the indoor profile simply mirrors it.
> A room dominated by *Stachybotrys* or *Penicillium* that isn't outside is a fail; a room that looks like the
> yard does is a pass. The outdoor sample is the ruler; never sample indoors without it.

### 3e. New section — "In Australia: NATA, ASTM and the moisture precondition"
> Two Australian specifics make a clearance defensible. First, **send samples to a NATA-accredited laboratory** —
> spore-trap air samples analysed by direct microscopy to **ASTM D7391** and tape/surface samples to **ASTM
> D7658**, under ISO/IEC 17025. A number from an unaccredited lab won't hold up in an insurance or legal dispute.
> The field method is standardised too: a spore-trap cassette run about **5 minutes at ~15 L/min**, pump on a
> tripod roughly **1.5–2 m** off the floor, paired with the outdoor reference and a **chain-of-custody** form.
> Second, **dryness is a precondition, not a separate box:** the materials must be back to the documented **dry
> standard** and the moisture source permanently fixed *before* you clear — excess moisture damages air quality
> even with no visible mould, and an undried cavity just regrows the problem behind your clean bill of health.

### 3f. New section — "Who signs off, and the report that proves it"
> The last question is *who decides.* The remediator's own check is a **Post-Remediation Evaluation (PRE)** — good
> quality control, but the contractor grading their own homework. The clearance that stands up is the
> **Post-Remediation Verification (PRV)**, performed by an **independent Indoor Environmental Professional** hired
> by the owner, not the remediator. Agree the pass criteria *before* work starts, and make the **handover report**
> the real deliverable: dated photos, the corrected moisture readings against the dry standard, the lab results
> indoor-vs-outdoor, and a plain statement that the source is fixed and the space is returned to Condition 1.
> That document is what lets the owner move back in — and what closes the job so it stays closed.

### 3g. "Interesting fact" hook
> The strangest rule in clearance testing: a freshly remediated room can show **more** mould spores in the air
> than it did when it was contaminated — and still pass. It sounds like a failure until you remember the ruler
> is *outside.* Clearance never asks "how many spores?" in isolation; it asks "does the inside look like a normal
> version of the outside?" On a high-pollen, high-mould spring day in Australia the outdoor count is enormous, so
> a genuinely clean indoor space that simply matches it can post a big number and be perfectly healthy. The
> number without the outdoor sample beside it is meaningless — which is why an air result with no outdoor
> reference tells you nothing at all.

## Block 5 · Considered & rejected
1. **Publishing numeric "pass" spore-count thresholds** (e.g. "<X CFU/m³ passes") — rejected as false precision:
   the sources are explicit that clearance is **qualitative vs the outdoor reference**, and there is no universal
   numeric limit; teaching a magic number would be actively wrong.
2. **A deep how-to on reading lab spore-trap slides** — trimmed; identification to genus is the NATA lab's job
   under ASTM D7391. The course teaches the technician to *commission and interpret* the comparison, not to do
   the microscopy.
3. **Framing clearance as the remediator's own final check** — deliberately inverted to the independent-IEP PRV
   as best practice, because self-clearance is exactly the conflict-of-interest the disputes turn on.
