## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "What the evidence actually says (be precise here)"
> It's easy to overstate mould. Here's the careful version restoration professionals should use with
> clients: the evidence now supports a *causal* link between indoor mould exposure and the development
> and worsening of asthma **in children**. For most other health claims — the dramatic ones people
> repeat online — the science shows an *association*, not proven cause, and there is no established
> dose-response line telling us how much exposure causes how much harm. That uncertainty is exactly
> why the health authorities land on a precautionary rule: you don't need to prove a specific illness
> to justify removing dampness and mould — minimising exposure is the correct response on its own.

### 3b. New section — "Why moisture is the lever you actually pull"
> Mould needs four things: nutrients, oxygen, the right temperature, and moisture. Indoors, the first
> three are almost always present — dust and building materials feed it, the air has oxygen, and room
> temperature suits it. Moisture is the only one you can realistically control, which is why every
> credible guideline treats moisture control as the primary intervention. And the moisture doesn't
> have to be visible: excess dampness harms health and damages buildings **even with no visible mould**,
> because it also drives dust mites, bacteria, and higher chemical emissions from wet materials.

### 3c. New section — "The Australian numbers (use these with clients)"
> - **One-third to one-half of Australian homes** are affected by mould.
> - A **family of four produces 7–22 kg of water vapour a day** just by living — breathing, cooking,
>   showering, drying clothes indoors. In a sealed modern home, that has to go somewhere.
> - Aim for indoor relative humidity of **30–60%** for comfort and **under ~70%** to hold back mould.
> - The damage can start **before you see any condensation** — problems begin below the dew point, and
>   the worst of it, *interstitial condensation*, happens hidden inside the wall build-up where nobody
>   looks until the plasterboard fails.

### 3d. "Interesting fact" hook
> The four people living in an average home breathe, cook and wash up to 22 kilograms of water into the
> air every day — heavier than a full carry-on bag of water vapour, daily. In an airtight modern build
> with the exhaust fans switched off, that's not a hygiene problem; it's a moisture-load problem, and
> it's why "just clean the mould" never fixes a recurring job.
