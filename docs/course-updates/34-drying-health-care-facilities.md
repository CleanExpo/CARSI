## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Drying a live hospital is different"
> Everything you know about structural drying still applies in a hospital — but the building fights back in a way
> a house never does. The people inside can be harmed by the very act of drying: disturb a damp ceiling void or
> run the wrong airflow near an oncology ward and you can aerosolise fungal spores that are lethal to an
> immunocompromised patient. So in healthcare, drying is not the first decision — **infection control is.** The
> psychrometrics don't change; the permission to touch anything does.

### 3b. New section — "The ICRA decides everything"
> Before a single air mover goes in, the facility runs an **Infection Control Risk Assessment (ICRA)** — a
> documented, multidisciplinary process that crosses the **patient risk group** (from low-risk offices up to
> *highest* — ICUs, oncology, transplant, theatres) with the **type of work** (minor to major), and lands on a
> **Class of Precautions from I to V.** A water loss, sewage or discovered mould automatically pushes you toward
> **Class IV or V** — the most stringent. As the restorer, you don't set the ICRA, but you **work to it**: it
> dictates your barriers, your pressure, your access route, your hours and your clearance. In Australia this
> sits under the **NHMRC infection-prevention guidelines** and the **Australasian Health Facility Guidelines**;
> the US ICRA 2.0 matrix is the clearest worked example of the method.

### 3c. New section — "Aspergillus and the immunocompromised patient"
> The specific danger has a name: ***Aspergillus*.** Its spores are everywhere, harmless to most of us and
> potentially fatal to a patient whose immune system is on the floor. Water-damaged building materials and the
> dust above ceiling tiles are reservoirs, and drying airflow is exactly the thing that lifts spores into the
> air. That's why, near high-risk patients, the rules are strict: **relocate** susceptible patients from
> adjacent areas, minimise any activity that aerosolises spores, and never assume "a bit of damp drywall" is a
> low-stakes job. In a hospital it isn't.

### 3d. New section — "The other half: waterborne pathogens"
> Everyone remembers the airborne risk; the waterborne one gets missed. Drying, flushing and fixture work
> **aerosolise water**, and that water can carry **Legionella.** Healthcare waterborne outbreaks tied to
> building works are severe — studies put the average at around **26 cases and 4 deaths**, dragging on a mean of
> **17 months.** A **Water-Management-for-Construction ICRA** (aligned to **ANSI/ASHRAE 188**, referenced in the
> Australian context) manages stagnation, flushing and any aerosol near patients or air intakes. If your drying
> plan moves or mists water, it belongs in that plan.

### 3e. New section — "Containment, HEPA and negative pressure"
> The engineering controls are the same trio, run to a higher standard:
> - **Barriers** sealed to the deck/slab above, not just the ceiling grid.
> - **Negative pressure** in the work zone relative to occupied areas — the poly should **billow inward**; if it
>   flutters outward, containment is lost, stop and fix it.
> - **HEPA** filtration (99.97% at 0.3 µm) on air scrubbers and any exhaust, ducted appropriately.
> - **HVAC discipline:** isolate affected systems; a water-touched duct is a spore highway and must be cleaned
>   and verified before restart.
> Damaged porous materials are double-bagged **inside** containment before they travel.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> In a hospital, the dangerous moment isn't the flood — it's the **drying.** The air movement that rescues a
> soaked wall is the same force that lifts *Aspergillus* spores out of the damp material and into the breathing
> space of a patient who can't fight them off. That's why healthcare drying inverts the normal order of work:
> the psychrometrics are identical to any other job, but you can't place a single fan until an **infection
> control risk assessment** has said where the air may go — and the clearance to hand the room back isn't "it
> looks dry", it's a lab result showing airborne fungal counts back near zero.
