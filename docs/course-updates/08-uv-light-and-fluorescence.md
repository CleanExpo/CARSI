## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "How fluorescence actually works (in one paragraph)"
> Ultraviolet light is just light at a wavelength too short for your eyes to see (roughly 10–400 nm,
> versus 400–700 nm for visible light). When UV hits certain substances, it knocks their electrons up to
> a higher energy state for an instant; as the electrons drop back down, they release that energy as
> *visible* light. That re-emission is fluorescence — and it's why a urine stain or a biological residue
> that's invisible in room light suddenly glows under a UV lamp. Different substances glow in different
> colours, which is what lets you tell them apart.

### 3b. New section — "365nm vs 395nm: the buying decision most people get wrong"
> Not all "UV lights" are equal. The cheap ones are **395nm** — and they give off a visible violet glow
> that actually fights you, washing out the faint fluorescence you're trying to see. The professional
> choice is **365nm ("true UV-A")**: it emits almost no visible light of its own, so the fluorescence
> stands out with far more contrast and lets you catch smaller, fainter traces. If you're buying a lamp
> to find contamination on a job, 365nm is worth the extra cost — 395nm will quietly cost you the faint
> stains that matter.

### 3c. New section — "What it finds — and the traps that fool beginners"
> - **Blood doesn't glow — it goes dark.** Haemoglobin *absorbs* UV, so dried blood reads as a dark
>   patch, not a bright one. Semen, saliva and some sweat residues are what fluoresce. Assuming "glow =
>   blood" is the classic rookie error.
> - **Not everything that glows is biological.** Fruit juices, honey, optical brighteners in laundry
>   detergent, and many fibres and finishes fluoresce too. A glow under UV is a *lead*, not a
>   confirmation — verify before you call it.
> - **The surface can hide the stain.** Substrates like denim or bright white cotton fluoresce so
>   strongly they can mask a real residue sitting on top.

### 3d. New section — "The contents/art use most techs never learn"
> On antiques, artwork and fine furnishings, UV fluorescence is a standard conservation check done
> *before* any cleaning. Old repairs, retouching and non-original layers fluoresce differently from the
> original — a repainted patch can light up while the original stays dark. Run the UV pass first and you
> avoid "cleaning off" a repair you didn't know was there, and you can document the true condition before
> you touch it.

### 3e. "Interesting fact" hook
> Everyone thinks blood glows under a UV light. It's the opposite — blood goes *dark*, because it soaks
> the UV up. The things that actually glow are the ones people don't expect: honey, orange juice, laundry
> residue, and the semen or saliva that a crime-scene lamp is really built to find.
