## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Urine changes chemistry over time"
> Pet urine isn't one substance — it's a chemistry experiment that runs for days after the accident. Fresh,
> it's about 95% water with urea, uric acid and salts, and it's **slightly acidic (pH ~5–6).** Then bacteria
> get to work: they convert the urea into **ammonia**, which is alkaline and produces that sharp,
> unmistakable smell, and the pH climbs. As the water evaporates, the **uric acid precipitates out into hard,
> water-resistant crystals** — urates — that bond to the carpet yarn, the latex backing and the foam pad.
> Understanding that timeline is the whole job: you're not cleaning a spill, you're dismantling a chemical
> deposit.

### 3b. New section — "Why the smell keeps coming back"
> The number-one complaint — "I cleaned it and the smell came back" — has one chemical explanation: **uric
> acid crystals.** They're insoluble in water (and in ammonia, and in most cleaners), so ordinary cleaning
> leaves them sitting in the substrate. Worse, they're **hygroscopic** — they *pull moisture out of humid
> air*, partly re-dissolve, and release the ammonia-like odour all over again. That's why a spot can seem
> perfectly clean for weeks and then reek during a rainy week, a humid summer, or an HVAC cycle. The crystals
> were never gone; they were waiting for moisture. This reactivation can continue for **years** in an
> untreated substrate.

### 3c. New section — "The three-layer problem"
> Treat pet urine as a surface stain and you'll fail, because it's a three-layer contamination:
> - **Face yarn** — what you can see.
> - **Backing and pad** — where the bulk of the urine wicks and pools. It spreads **6–18 inches beyond the
>   visible spot** and soaks downward.
> - **Subfloor** — plywood or concrete that absorbs what reaches it.
>
> Each layer needs the chemistry delivered to *its* depth. A surface spray never reaches the pad reservoir,
> which is exactly why the odour survives a "clean." Map the real footprint first (below), then decide how
> deep you have to go.

### 3d. New section — "Enzyme then oxidiser — in that order"
> Because water can't dissolve uric acid, you need biology and chemistry in sequence:
> 1. **Alkaline pre-spray** — a surfactant to lift surface urine and start loosening the matrix.
> 2. **Enzyme treatment** — protease/lipase enzymes digest the organic matrix and the uric-acid precursors,
>    converting them to odourless byproducts (CO₂, water, simple inorganics). *There's nothing left to
>    reactivate* — but only if the enzyme reaches the contamination depth and stays wet long enough to finish.
> 3. **Oxidiser** — neutralises residual bacterial byproducts and helps with discolouration (colour-test first).
> 4. **Acid rinse** — returns the fibre to near-neutral pH, reducing residue and re-soiling.
>
> Skip any one of these and the smell is back within about a month. Enzymes are digesters, not perfumes — give
> them dwell time, moisture and depth, or they only half-work.

### 3e. New section — "The heat mistake that sets odour forever"
> Here's the error that turns a fixable job into a permanent one: **applying heat before treating.** Steam
> cleaning or hot-water extraction run *first* denatures the proteins and **thermally bonds the uric acid
> deeper into the material** — heat doesn't remove the odour, it *fixes it in place.* Treat the chemistry
> first (enzyme dwell), *then* extract. Two more traps: **over-wetting** drives contamination 6–18 inches
> further out and down, and the **DIY vinegar/baking-soda** the customer already tried leaves the uric acid
> intact while making your job harder. Tell customers to blot only and wait.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> The reason a cleaned pet stain smells again on a rainy day is genuinely strange chemistry: as urine dries
> it forms **uric-acid crystals that water can't dissolve** — and those crystals are *hygroscopic*, so they
> pull moisture straight out of humid air, re-dissolve, and release the smell all over again. The spot was
> never clean; the crystals were just waiting for the weather. And the classic mistake makes it permanent —
> steam-cleaning before treating literally *cooks* the odour into the fibres.
