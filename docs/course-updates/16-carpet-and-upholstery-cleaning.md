## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "CHAT: the four levers of every clean"
> Every cleaning result comes from four factors working together — remember them as **CHAT**:
> - **Chemical action** separates and emulsifies soil.
> - **Heat** speeds the chemistry up.
> - **Agitation** distributes product and physically frees soil from the fibre.
> - **Time** (dwell) lets the chemistry work.
>
> The rule that makes you a professional: **if you dial one down, dial another up.** Can't use heat on a
> heat-sensitive fibre? Give it more dwell time and agitation. Can't agitate a delicate pile? Lean on
> chemistry and time. CHAT is the dial-set you're balancing on every single job.

### 3b. New section — "Identify the fibre first — or pay for it"
> Fibre identification is the first decision on every job, because the fibre dictates your chemistry, heat,
> pressure, method — and whether the carpet is safe to clean with water at all. The most expensive mistake
> in the trade is cleaning a **wool** rug as if it were nylon: high-alkaline traffic-lane chemistry and hot
> water cause browning, colour bleed and shrinkage — permanent damage, and a cheque you write to the
> customer. When you're not sure, the **burn test** on a single fibre from a hidden edge is definitive:
> - **Nylon / polyester** *melt* to a hard bead (nylon smells chemical/celery; polyester sweeter, darker bead).
> - **Wool** *chars*, self-extinguishes, smells of **burnt hair**, and leaves soft **crushable ash** — switch
>   immediately to cool water and neutral, wool-safe chemistry.
> - **Olefin** melts fast, shrinks from the flame, smells waxy — watch your heat.
>
> When in doubt, default to the gentlest safe approach; you can always clean harder on a second pass, but you
> can't undo browning or shrinkage.

### 3c. New section — "pH and the wool trap"
> Most soils are acidic, so most cleaners are alkaline to neutralise and suspend them. But the fibre sets
> the ceiling: **nylon** handles a wide pH band (~5–10), **olefin** shrugs off high alkalinity, while
> **wool, silk and stain-resistant nylon must be cleaned near neutral** (below ~8). On wool and dyes,
> alkaline damage can be almost instant and irreversible. Two rules follow: match the pH to the fibre, and
> **finish every job with the fibre back in the 5.5–7 range** — use an acid rinse to neutralise alkaline
> residue, or you leave the carpet prone to rapid re-soiling and, on naturals, yellowing.

### 3d. New section — "Hot water extraction vs encapsulation (run both, on purpose)"
> These aren't rivals — they're a schedule.
> - **Hot water extraction** injects heated solution (best at **60–70 °C**; above ~80 °C you risk distorting
>   nylon) and vacuums it straight back out with the soil. It removes **90–95%** of deep-bedded soil and
>   allergens — the real deep clean — but leaves carpet wet for hours.
> - **Encapsulation** wraps soil in a polymer that dries brittle in 30–60 minutes and vacuums out over the
>   next few days. It removes **65–70%** — appearance maintenance, not deep cleaning — but the floor is
>   walkable almost immediately.
>
> The professional pattern: **quarterly extraction for the deep clean, encapsulation between times for
> appearance.** Monthly extraction over-wets carpet, risks mould in poorly ventilated buildings, and the
> detergent residue actually accelerates re-soiling.

### 3e. New section — "Why spots come back: wicking"
> A spot you removed reappears a day later — that's **wicking.** Two things cause it: soil or spot residue
> left deep in the pile or backing, and **capillary action** drawing that residue up to the surface as the
> carpet dries. It's worst on **polyester**, which doesn't absorb the cleaning water so it runs into the
> backing and carries soil back up. Beat it with complete pre-vacuuming and extraction (don't leave residue
> for the water to carry), lower psi on non-absorbent fibres, and **fast drying** — soils don't wick once
> the carpet is dry. And remember **olefin**: it repels water stains but holds oily soil, so grey lanes need
> a strong surfactant and good extraction, never just more water.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> A single strand of carpet, pulled from under the sofa and touched with a flame, tells a pro everything.
> If it melts into a hard little bead, it's synthetic. If it **chars, curls away from the flame, and smells
> like burnt hair** — that's wool, and it just changed the entire job: cool water, gentle chemistry, and no
> room for error, because cleaning wool like nylon is the fastest way to turn a carpet clean into a carpet claim.
