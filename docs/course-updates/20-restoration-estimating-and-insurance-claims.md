## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "The estimate is a shared language, not a bid"
> The first thing to understand about insurance restoration estimating is that **you're not writing a bid —
> you're writing in a shared language.** In North America that language is **Xactimate**: the carrier's
> adjuster, any independent adjuster, and you all price from the **same regional database**, updated
> regularly. Because everyone sees the same rates, **nobody argues about the price of a line item — they
> argue about which line items belong.** That reframes the whole job: your leverage isn't haggling over
> rates, it's proving *scope*. (Xactimate is the North-American standard; if you estimate in another market,
> the platform may differ, but the scope-not-rate principle carries.)

### 3b. New section — "Scope of loss vs scope of work (where the money hides)"
> Two documents look similar and aren't:
> - **Scope of loss** — the *adjuster's* damage assessment and what the carrier will pay. On residential
>   claims it's often only **50–65% of the actual repair cost**, because it's built from a 30–90 minute
>   walk-through of *visible* damage.
> - **Scope of work** — what *you* actually have to build to return the property to pre-loss condition. It
>   should reflect **100%** of the required work.
>
> The gap between the two is not an error to argue about — it's **where legitimate supplement revenue
> lives.** Hidden moisture behind intact drywall, saturated insulation, attic infiltration: none of it was
> visible in a 45-minute inspection, so none of it is in the first estimate. Your thorough scope, with a
> moisture meter and thermal camera, is what closes the gap.

### 3c. New section — "The sketch decides the approval"
> The single biggest predictor of whether an estimate gets approved cleanly is **sketch quality.** A clean
> sketch — every room measured on-site (laser, then verified), drawn to scale, labelled by purpose (not
> "Room 1"), with openings drawn and affected materials labelled — is approved with minimal questions. A
> messy sketch, wrong by even six inches, throws off every quantity downstream and triggers rejection
> cycles. Sketch **at the loss site, not from memory back at the office**, and tie a photo to every room.

### 3d. New section — "Supplements are normal — and where the money is"
> A supplement isn't an admission you got it wrong; it's the standard mechanism for adding damage discovered
> after the initial estimate — and **almost every job has at least one.** Companies with a weak supplement
> process leave **5–15% of revenue** on the table; well-documented supplements average a **34.4% increase**
> in residential RCV. The workflow that works:
> 1. **Document** the additional damage with photos and notes the moment you find it (especially during
>    tear-off — photograph the rotted decking *before* you replace it).
> 2. **Write it in Xactimate within 48 hours**, correct line-item codes, accurate quantities.
> 3. **Submit through the proper channel** (carrier portal, adjuster, TPA system).
> 4. **Follow up weekly** and track every supplement to closure.
>
> The desk adjuster reviewing it was never on site — everything they know comes from your photos and your
> Xactimate file. A handwritten list gets ignored; a proper Xactimate supplement gets compared and approved.

### 3e. New section — "Documentation wins disputes (not argument)"
> When a carrier pushes back on a line item, the answer is never a louder email — it's **documentation.** For
> each contested item, assemble four things: a **photo** of the affected material, a **measurable indicator**
> (a moisture reading, a count, a measurement), an **IICRC standard reference** (S500 for water, S520 for
> mould, S700 for fire), and a clear **note** — in Xactimate, an **F9 note** attached to the line item
> ("hardwood runs continuously into the unaffected hallway; matching board discontinued; full-run
> replacement required for match"). A line item with photos and a standard behind it is hard to dismiss; one
> with nothing is dismissed as routine. And don't forget the quiet omissions — **O&P** when three or more
> trades are coordinated, and **detach-and-reset (D/S)** items for undamaged fixtures in the way.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> On a typical home water or fire claim, the insurer's first estimate covers only about **half to two-thirds
> of the real repair cost** — and the missing third isn't a fight over prices, because the carrier and the
> contractor price from the *same* database. It's a gap in **scope**: the damage an adjuster simply couldn't
> see on a 45-minute walk-through. The estimator who documents what the walk-through missed is the one who
> gets the property fully restored — and fully paid.
