## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Category is about contamination, not the source"
> The first thing to get right on any water loss is the **Category** — and it's a measure of how
> *contaminated* the water is, not simply where it came from:
> - **Category 1** — clean water from a sanitary source (a supply line, a tap).
> - **Category 2** ("grey water") — significantly contaminated, enough to cause illness if ingested
>   (a washing-machine overflow, a dishwasher discharge).
> - **Category 3** ("black water") — grossly contaminated (sewage, rising flood water, or *any* lower
>   category that has been left long enough to degrade).
>
> That last point is the one beginners miss: **category isn't frozen at the moment of loss.** Clean
> Category 1 water sitting against wet materials climbs to Category 2 and then Category 3 as microbial
> load grows — time, temperature and the material it's soaking into all push it up the scale. The clock
> is part of the assessment.

### 3b. New section — "The four Classes: how much water, and how hard to remove"
> Where Category is about *contamination*, **Class** is about the **evaporation load** — how much water is
> present and how tightly the materials hold it:
> - **Class 1** — the least water; little absorption, fast to dry.
> - **Class 2** — a whole room affected; water has wicked up the walls a short way.
> - **Class 3** — the greatest amount, usually from **above** (ceilings, walls and insulation saturated) —
>   the whole structure is loaded.
> - **Class 4** — **specialty drying**: water bound into low-permeance materials like hardwood, plaster,
>   brick and concrete, which release moisture slowly and need targeted methods (heat, low GPP, sometimes
>   injection systems).
>
> Category tells you how safe the water is; Class tells you how big and how stubborn the drying job is.

### 3c. New section — "What changed in S500 (the 2026 revision)"
> If your training pre-dates 2026, the standard you're working to has moved. The **ANSI/IICRC S500 2026
> revision** is the most significant update in nearly a decade, and four changes matter on the job:
> - **Documentation is now central, not supporting.** A moisture map, atmospheric readings and material
>   moisture-content readings must form a **continuous, timestamped record from first notice through
>   completion** — the evidence trail *is* part of the standard of care now.
> - **Category and Class definitions are sharper.** The line for when Category 2 becomes Category 3, and
>   the criteria for Class 4, are written more tightly — a well-documented call holds up when a carrier or
>   TPA challenges the scope; a vague one gets picked apart.
> - **The drying goal must be set and proven.** Document the goal at the start, state how you'll verify it,
>   and tie the final reading back to it.
> - **New methods are recognised.** IoT continuous monitoring now counts as acceptable documentation
>   alongside manual psychrometric readings.

### 3d. New section — "When drying becomes remediation (the S500 ↔ S520 line)"
> A water job and a mould job are governed by two different standards — S500 for water mitigation, S520 for
> mould remediation — and the 2026 revision sharpened the line between them. The trigger to cross from
> "drying" into "remediation" is a combination of **time elapsed, contamination indicators, and the
> materials affected.** Cross it and the job changes: you now need containment, upgraded PPE, and a
> different documentation set. Knowing where that line sits — and documenting the moment you reach it — is
> what separates a defensible scope from an argument with the carrier.

### 3e. Field-discipline box
> - **Assess Category on the actual site conditions**, not the weather headline — a storm loss isn't
>   automatically Category 3 (IICRC 2026 position statement).
> - **Re-check Category over time** — clean water degrades; yesterday's Cat 1 can be today's Cat 3.
> - **Timestamp everything** — moisture map, atmospheric and material readings, FNOL to completion.
> - **Watch for the S520 trigger** and re-scope the moment drying becomes remediation.
> - **Flag PFAS-exposure losses** — they can need testing and disposal well beyond standard Cat 2/3.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> "Clean" water doesn't stay clean. The exact same burst-pipe water that's a straightforward Category 1 job
> on day one becomes a Category 2 — and then a contaminated Category 3 — if it's left sitting in the
> building. On a water loss, the calendar is part of the diagnosis: category isn't just *what spilled*,
> it's *how long it's been there.*
