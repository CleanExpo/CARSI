## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Deodorising isn't one thing: the four agent classes"
> Before you reach for a machine, understand the *agents*, because most odour failures come from using the
> wrong class:
> - **Masking agents** — fragrances that cover the smell with a stronger, pleasant one. They fix the
>   *psychology* of odour, never the source. Useful to buy comfort while you neutralise; useless as the cure.
> - **Absorption / filtration** — **activated charcoal** genuinely *adsorbs* odour molecules onto its huge
>   surface area. (The supermarket carpet powders are mostly baking soda — they absorb a little humidity
>   and destroy nothing, and they pack into the pile and wear the carpet.)
> - **Pairing agents** — chemicals that *combine* with airborne odour molecules, making them heavy enough
>   to drop out of the air onto surfaces you'll then clean. Think of it as "washing the air."
> - **Disinfectants / sanitisers** — kill the bacteria and fungi that *produce* the smell in the first place.
>
> The professional move is to match the agent to the odour, and never let a masking fragrance stand in for
> real neutralisation.

### 3b. New section — "Oxidising the odour: fog, hydroxyl, ozone"
> For odour that has soaked into the structure, three oxidising methods dominate — and they are **not**
> interchangeable:
> - **Thermal fogging** — a deodoriser heated into a fine 0.5–5 micron fog that chases smoke into the same
>   wall voids and subfloors it hid in. Powerful, but the building must be **completely evacuated** (people,
>   pets, plants), HVAC off and sealed, and ventilated before re-entry.
> - **Hydroxyl generation** — UV lamps make hydroxyl radicals, the same "detergent of the atmosphere" the
>   sun produces. They break odour VOCs down to CO₂ and water, have a **half-life under a second**, and are
>   **safe to run in an occupied building 24/7** while your crew works. Slower — plan **2–4 days** on a
>   heavy job.
> - **Ozone** — fast and aggressive (a few hours), but **toxic**: it irritates lungs and oxidises rubber,
>   plants, artwork and some electronics. **Vacant, evacuated structures only**, with an off-gassing period
>   before anyone goes back in.

### 3c. New section — "The occupancy rule that decides the tool"
> Here is the rule that keeps you safe and out of trouble: **the choice is determined by occupancy status
> and material sensitivity — not by which machine is on the truck.** If the building has people, pets,
> plants, rubber, artwork or sensitive electronics in it, **hydroxyl is the only acceptable option.** If the
> structure is confirmed vacant and fully evacuated with sensitive materials protected, ozone's speed can be
> the right call. Running ozone in an occupied space is not a judgement call — it is outside acceptable
> practice.

### 3d. New section — "Deodorise the source, not just the air"
> The most common reason odour comes back is treating the air and ignoring the source. A revealing field
> study makes the point: as a hydroxyl generator ran, the smell sometimes got **stronger** for a while —
> because the radicals were breaking soot down off the walls, releasing trapped odour as they worked. That
> rising smell is a **quality-control signal**: if the odour climbs while you deodorise, charred material is
> still in the building and needs to be physically removed. Deodorising equipment supplements source
> removal and ventilation — it does not replace them.

### 3e. "Interesting fact" hook
> The safest odour-killer in the restorer's kit is a copy of something the sky makes for free. Hydroxyl
> radicals — the "detergent of the atmosphere" the sun creates from sunlight and water vapour — are more
> reactive than ozone yet vanish in under a second, which is exactly why a generator can run in a room full
> of people while ozone would send everyone outside.
