## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "Know the stone before you touch it"
> The first question on any stone job is chemical, not cosmetic: **is it calcareous or siliceous?**
> - **Calcareous** stones — marble, travertine, limestone, onyx — are calcium carbonate, and they **react
>   with acid.** Any acid touching them dissolves the surface.
> - **Siliceous** stones — granite, quartzite, slate, sandstone — are silicate-based and far more
>   acid-tolerant (though acids still aren't recommended).
>
> Get this wrong and you can't clean your way out of it. A quick field check: a drop of a mild acidic
> cleaner on a hidden calcareous spot will fizz and dull; on siliceous stone it won't. When unsure, treat
> it as the more sensitive calcareous stone.

### 3b. New section — "Etching is not staining (and that's the whole point)"
> The single most important distinction in stone care: **a stain sits on the stone; an etch *is* the stone
> — gone.** Acid reacts with calcium carbonate and dissolves the polished surface, leaving a dull, rough
> patch. A few drops of vinegar left on marble for ten minutes will do it. Because it's physical damage,
> **no cleaning restores it** — only mechanical honing and re-polishing. Tell them apart: a **stain** changes
> the *colour*; an **etch** changes the *sheen*. And the products that cause it are the ones people trust —
> vinegar (pH ~2.4), lemon, CLR, "natural" citrus cleaners, and most bathroom/grout cleaners. On stone the
> rule is **pH-neutral only**, and on a fresh spill you **blot, never wipe** — wiping spreads the acid.

### 3c. New section — "The grout-cleaner trap"
> Here's the mistake that makes a clean floor look *worse*: reaching for a normal grout cleaner on a marble
> or travertine floor. Grout cleaners are deliberately acidic or alkaline to dissolve deposits — and they
> etch the stone on either side of every joint, leaving a dull line tracing each grout line across the whole
> floor. Two safe paths: use a **stone-safe, pH-neutral grout cleaner**, scrub the grout only, and rinse
> before it can creep onto the tile; or, on tolerant stone like slate and granite, spend three minutes
> **masking the stone** beside the grout first. For mould in grout on calcareous stone, dilute **3% hydrogen
> peroxide** is a gentler compromise than commercial acidic mould cleaners.

### 3d. New section — "'Sealers' don't seal"
> The word misleads everyone. Stone "sealers" are **impregnators** — they line the pores to *repel* liquid
> and slow absorption, buying you minutes-to-hours to wipe up a spill. They do **not** make stone
> stain-proof, and critically they **do not stop acid etching** — a sealer only slows the liquid reaching
> the stone; it can't stop the acid reaction once it does. Don't reseal on a calendar; use the **water-drop
> test** — put a few drops down and watch: if it beads, the sealer's working; if it soaks in and darkens
> the stone, it's time to reseal. And never put a topical "gloss/wax/floor-finish" coating on natural stone.

### 3e. New section — "Efflorescence: it comes from the water"
> That white powder blooming on grout isn't dirt — it's **mineral salts** carried up from inside the
> installation by moisture and left on the surface as the water evaporates. It needs three things together:
> soluble salts (Portland-cement grout supplies them), moisture, and a path to the surface. The instinct
> to wash it off makes it worse — the water just dissolves the salts and re-deposits them. **Dry-remove it**
> (dust-mop/vacuum, repeat as it keeps drying) and **find and fix the moisture source.** Only stubborn
> deposits justify a careful acidic grout cleaner — and acids erode grout and threaten adjacent stone, so
> they're a last resort, never routine.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> The most destructive thing in most kitchens for a marble bench is the bottle labelled "natural": vinegar.
> A few drops left for ten minutes chemically dissolve the polished surface and leave a dull etch that no
> amount of scrubbing will ever bring back — only a professional grinding the stone down and re-polishing
> it can. On marble, the "stain" you're looking at often isn't on the stone at all; it's a piece of the
> stone that's gone.
