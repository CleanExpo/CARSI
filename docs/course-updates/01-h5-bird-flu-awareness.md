## Why this course needs updating (the load-bearing change)

Any H5 module written before **June 2026** almost certainly states that *"Australia is the only
continent free of H5 bird flu."* That statement is now **false**. In June 2026 Australia
confirmed its **first-ever detections of H5N1 high-pathogenicity avian influenza (clade 2.3.4.4b)
in wild birds**. This is the single most important correction for the course — it changes the
framing from "prepare for a threat that hasn't arrived" to "a detected incursion is underway in
wild seabirds; here is the current picture and your role."

## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New callout — "Current situation (as at July 2026)" — insert at the top of the course

> **Current situation — this is a live, evolving incursion.**
> In June 2026, Australia recorded its first detections of H5 bird flu (H5N1, clade 2.3.4.4b) in
> wild birds — beginning with two sick seabirds near Esperance, Western Australia. As at 9 July
> 2026 there are eight confirmed or presumed cases across Western Australia, South Australia and
> New South Wales, all in individual migratory seabirds. There is no detection in commercial
> poultry and no mass wildlife die-off in Australia at this stage, and the Australian CDC assesses
> the risk to people as low. Treat the figures in this lesson as a snapshot: check the DAFF bird-flu
> page and Wildlife Health Australia for the current count before you rely on it on a job.

*(word count: 118)*

### 3b. Corrected framing paragraph — replaces any "Australia is H5-free" statement

> For years, Australia was the one continent H5N1 hadn't reached — the virus spread to every other
> continent, Antarctica included, before it arrived here. That changed in June 2026. For a
> restoration, IAQ or facility professional, the practical shift is this: bird-contaminated sites,
> roof voids, plant rooms and water-ingress jobs involving bird droppings now sit against a
> confirmed-present pathogen, not a hypothetical one. The controls below are unchanged in kind —
> they matter more now because the exposure pathway is real.

*(word count: 92)*

### 3c. New "interesting fact" learning hook (engagement, non-assessable)

> Australia held out longer than anywhere on the planet. This H5N1 clade tore through the Americas,
> Europe, Africa, Asia and even sub-Antarctic wildlife, and Australia was the final continent it
> hadn't reached — until a handful of migratory seabirds carried it across the southern coastline
> in mid-2026. Geography bought time; it didn't buy immunity.

*(word count: 58)*

### 3d. Practical "your role" box — facility/restoration angle

> **On the job, if you encounter multiple sick or dead birds:**
> 1. Do not touch or approach them, and keep others back.
> 2. Record the location; take a photo or short video only if you can do so safely from distance.
> 3. Call the **Emergency Animal Disease Hotline: 1800 675 888**.
> 4. Where a site involves bird droppings or carcasses (roof voids, ledges, plant rooms), use
>    standard and transmission-based precautions and appropriate PPE, and keep unprotected people out.
> 5. If you work at a site under an active response, seasonal influenza vaccination is recommended —
>    it won't stop H5 directly, but it lowers the chance of a co-infection that could let the virus
>    reassort.

*(word count: 116)*

## Block 5 · Considered & rejected

1. **Alarmist lede** ("Bird flu has arrived — are your sites safe?") — **rejected.** Overstates
   personal risk the CDC rates as low; erodes CARSI's standards-authority voice and would age badly
   if the incursion stays confined to seabirds.
2. **Hard CEC-hours framing** ("earn 1 CEC on avian biosecurity") — **rejected.** This is a non-CEC
   course; CEC framing is opt-in + fail-closed per estate rule. No CEC/IICRC claim is made.
3. **Static case-count in body text** — **rejected** in favour of the "snapshot, re-check the
   source" callout (3a), because the count changes weekly and a hardcoded number would be wrong
   within days.
