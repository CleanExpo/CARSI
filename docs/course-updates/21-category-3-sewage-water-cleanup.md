## Block 3 · Draft content

### 3a. New section — "What Category 3 actually is"
> Category 3 water — "black water" — is **grossly contaminated**: it can carry pathogens, toxins and other
> harmful agents. The obvious sources are sewage back-ups, rising flood water and ground/river water. The
> less obvious one catches people out: **any Category 1 or 2 water left in place long enough degrades into
> Category 3** as microbial load climbs. So "black water" isn't about how dark or smelly it is — it's a
> contamination judgement, and time is part of it.

### 3b. New section — "The trap-side test (get the category right first)"
> Before any protocol, get the category right, because it changes everything downstream. The cleanest
> example is a toilet: water from the **supply line** (clean, before the bowl) is **Category 1**; water from
> an **overflow beyond the trap** is **Category 3 — regardless of what it looks or smells like.** Confirm
> the source before you act. Call a Category 1 loss "Category 3" and you needlessly rip out restorable
> materials and inflate the claim; call a Category 3 "Category 1" and you leave a genuine health hazard in
> the walls and take on the liability. One question — *which side of the trap?* — sets the whole job.

### 3c. New section — "Porous out, non-porous cleaned"
> The removal rule on a Cat 3 loss is far more aggressive than on clean water, and it's decided by porosity:
> - **Porous materials that contacted the water are not restorable** — drywall, insulation, **carpet
>   padding**, ceiling tiles, untreated wood, and stuffed/upholstered contents come out. Carpet saturated
>   with Category 3 water is gone.
> - **Non-porous surfaces** — ceramic tile, concrete, sealed metal — can be **kept after verified
>   disinfection.**
> - **Framing** (wood/steel) can often be **saved** if you can get direct access and thoroughly clean and
>   sanitise it, paying special attention to joints and where the base plate meets the subfloor.
>
> Remove porous material to about **12 inches (30 cm) beyond the visible contamination line**, and bag the
> debris in **6-mil poly** before it leaves the containment.

### 3d. New section — "Clean first, then disinfect"
> The order matters and beginners reverse it. Decontamination is achieved **first by cleaning** — detergent
> cleaning physically removes the bioburden: the contaminants, the microorganisms, and the food source they
> live on. **Only then** do you apply an **EPA-registered disinfectant.** Spraying antimicrobial over
> uncleaned contamination is theatre — you can't disinfect a surface still coated in the organic material
> that's feeding the microbes. Clean it, *then* treat it, and collect the waste water for disposal to a
> **treated sewer — never to stormwater.**

### 3e. New section — "Contain so you don't re-contaminate"
> On a Cat 3 loss, containment isn't just to protect the rest of the building — it's to protect the work you
> just did. Bacteria are tiny (about 0.1–10 microns) and go airborne easily during demolition and cleaning,
> so two rules follow. First, **build containment with negative air** when the loss affects more than about
> 10 square feet of occupied space or where the HVAC could spread it — and shut and seal the HVAC. Second,
> **clean and decontaminate before you run air movers**, and never point airflow in a direction that would
> carry contamination into an area you've already cleaned. Even on a whole-structure loss, negative air is
> what stops a clean room becoming a dirty one again. PPE throughout: at least an **N95**, splash goggles,
> waterproof gloves and boots.

### 3f. "Interesting fact" hook
> The exact same puddle from an overflowing toilet can be a routine clean-water job or a full biohazard
> removal — and the only thing that decides it is **which side of the trap the water came from.** Supply-line
> water is Category 1; anything past the trap is black water no matter how clear it looks. That single
> question determines whether the drywall stays or goes — which is why the pros confirm the source before
> they touch a thing.
