# How to Get on Government Restoration Panels

## A Practical Guide for Australian Restoration Contractors

## About This Guide

Government restoration work represents some of the most reliable, high-value contracts available to Australian restoration companies. State and federal agencies manage thousands of buildings — schools, hospitals, courthouses, defence facilities, public housing — all of which require restoration services when water damage, mould, fire, or storms strike.

Getting onto a government panel means predictable revenue, professional credibility, and a competitive moat that smaller operators struggle to cross.

This guide walks you through the requirements, certifications, and application process for pursuing government restoration panel positions across Australia.

## Chapter 1: Understanding Government Procurement

### How Government Panels Work

Government agencies at all levels (federal, state, and local) use **standing offer arrangements** (also called panels, registers, or approved supplier lists) to pre-qualify contractors. When restoration work is needed, the agency contacts panel members directly — bypassing the open-tender process entirely.

**Why panels matter:**

- **Faster engagement** — No need to compete for every individual job
- **Predictable pipeline** — Panel members receive work on rotation or by capability
- **Established rates** — Panel rates are agreed up front through the procurement process, rather than renegotiated job by job
- **Payment reliability** — Government agencies pay on agreed terms (typically 30 days)

## Chapter 2: Required IICRC Certifications

### Why IICRC Credentials Are Non-Negotiable

Government procurement officers evaluate contractors on documented competency. IICRC (Institute of Inspection Cleaning and Restoration Certification) is a globally recognised standard for restoration qualifications, and many Australian government tender specifications for restoration work reference IICRC standards and certifications.

**One important distinction before you plan your training:** IICRC certification itself (WRT, ASD, FSRT and so on) is obtained only through IICRC-approved schools and examinations. CARSI is an IICRC CEC Accredited provider — CARSI courses earn Continuing Education Credits (CECs) toward maintaining an existing IICRC certification. CARSI courses do not grant IICRC certification.

**What procurement officers look for:**

- IICRC certification numbers for key personnel
- Evidence of continuing education (CECs)
- Alignment with IICRC S500 (water), S520 (mould), and S300 (carpet) standards
- Current certification status (not expired)

### Essential Certifications by Service Type

#### Tier 1: Foundation (Minimum for Any Panel Application)

| Certification                | Code | Why It's Required                                                                                               |
| ---------------------------- | ---- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Water Restoration Technician | WRT  | Foundation for all water damage work. Commonly referenced in government restoration panel specifications.        |
| Applied Structural Drying    | ASD  | Demonstrates advanced drying science knowledge. Required for complex structural drying projects.                |

#### Tier 2: Specialist (Broadens Panel Eligibility)

| Certification                             | Code | Why It's Required                                                                             |
| ----------------------------------------- | ---- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Carpet Repair & Reinstallation Technician | CRT  | Required for soft flooring restoration in government buildings (schools, offices, aged care). |
| Odour Control Technician                  | OCT  | Specialist skill for fire/smoke damage, sewage events, and environmental odour issues.        |
| Commercial Carpet Maintenance Technician  | CCT  | High-volume commercial maintenance for government office buildings.                           |

#### Tier 3: Advanced (Premium Panel Positions)

| Certification                              | Code | Why It's Required                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------ | ---- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Mould Remediation (aligned to IICRC S520)  | —    | Critical for health-sensitive environments (hospitals, schools, aged care). Some states require specific mould licences. |
| Fire & Smoke Restoration Technician (FSRT) | FSRT | Required for fire damage panels. Often separate from water damage panels.                                                |

### CEC (Continuing Education Credit) Requirements

IICRC certifications require ongoing education to maintain active status:

- **14 CECs per certification** over a defined cycle
- CECs must be earned through IICRC-approved CE providers
- CARSI is an IICRC CEC Accredited provider — its courses earn CECs toward maintaining your existing IICRC certification
- Government panels may audit your CEC records — keep them current

**Pro tip:** A CARSI yearly membership ($795 AUD/year, per learner) provides access to all published CARSI courses, and team plans are available for crews and larger businesses — see carsi.com.au/pricing for current options. Online, self-paced CEC training also avoids the travel and downtime costs of face-to-face sessions.

## Chapter 3: WHS Compliance Checklist

### Work Health and Safety Requirements

Government agencies are bound by the _Work Health and Safety Act 2011_ (harmonised across most states) and take WHS compliance extremely seriously. Your panel application will be assessed against these requirements.

### Documentation Required

#### 1. WHS Management System

- [ ] Written WHS policy signed by a director or principal
- [ ] WHS management plan specific to restoration work
- [ ] Hazard identification and risk assessment procedures
- [ ] Incident reporting and investigation procedures
- [ ] Emergency response plan
- [ ] Worker consultation and communication procedures

#### 2. Safe Work Method Statements (SWMS)

You will need SWMS for every high-risk construction work activity. For restoration, this typically includes:

- [ ] Working at heights (above 2 metres)
- [ ] Working in confined spaces (subfloor, ceiling cavities, tanks)
- [ ] Handling hazardous chemicals (antimicrobials, biocides, sealants)
- [ ] Asbestos awareness and management (pre-2000 buildings)
- [ ] Electrical safety (working near water and electrical systems)
- [ ] Manual handling (equipment, extraction, furniture relocation)
- [ ] Hot works (thermal fogging, welding during fire restoration)
- [ ] Working near traffic or public areas

#### 3. Insurance Certificates

| Insurance Type         | Minimum Cover                | Notes                                       |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Public liability       | $20M (government standard)   | Some panels require $50M                    |
| Professional indemnity | $5M–10M                      | Required for assessment/consulting work     |
| Workers' compensation  | State statutory requirements | Must be current for all workers             |
| Motor vehicle          | Comprehensive fleet cover    | Required if vehicles enter government sites |
| Pollution liability    | $5M–10M                      | Increasingly required for mould/hazmat work |

#### 4. Personnel Qualifications

- [ ] IICRC certifications for all technicians (not just the business owner)
- [ ] White Card (General Construction Induction) for all site workers
- [ ] Working with Children Check (for school/childcare facility work)
- [ ] National Police Check (for sensitive sites — defence, justice, healthcare)
- [ ] First Aid Certificate (current, within 3 years)
- [ ] Asbestos Awareness training (mandatory for pre-2000 buildings)
- [ ] Confined Space Entry certification (where applicable)

#### 5. Environmental Management

- [ ] Waste management plan (restoration generates significant waste)
- [ ] Hazardous waste disposal procedures and licensed waste contractor details
- [ ] Environmental incident response procedure
- [ ] Water usage and discharge plan (relevant for flood restoration)

### State-Specific Requirements

| State   | Additional Requirement                                        |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **NSW** | SafeWork NSW notification for certain high-risk work          |
| **VIC** | WorkSafe Victoria registration; EPA licence for certain waste |
| **QLD** | QBCC licence for building work above $3,300                   |
| **WA**  | Building Services Board registration                          |
| **SA**  | SafeWork SA notification requirements                         |
| **TAS** | WorkSafe Tasmania registration                                |

## Chapter 4: The Application Process

### Step 1: Register on Procurement Platforms

Create accounts on all relevant platforms (see Chapter 1). Complete your supplier profile thoroughly — procurement officers use these profiles to shortlist candidates.

**Profile essentials:**

- ABN and company registration details
- Capability statement (see below)
- Insurance certificates of currency
- IICRC certification evidence
- WHS management system summary
- Reference letters from previous clients

### Step 2: Prepare Your Capability Statement

A capability statement is a 2–4 page document that summarises your company's qualifications. Government procurement officers review hundreds of these — make yours scannable and evidence-based.

**Structure:**

1. **Company overview** — Who you are, years in operation, locations serviced
2. **Core services** — List each restoration service with IICRC standard alignment
3. **Personnel qualifications** — Table of staff, their IICRC certifications, and CEC status
4. **Equipment inventory** — Major equipment categories (extractors, dehumidifiers, air scrubbers, moisture meters, thermal imaging cameras)
5. **Insurance summary** — Policy types, coverage amounts, insurer names
6. **WHS credentials** — WHS management system accreditation, incident record, safety awards
7. **Past performance** — 3–5 relevant project summaries (scope, value, outcome, client reference)
8. **Capacity** — Response time commitment, geographic coverage, staffing levels

### Step 3: Monitor and Respond to Panel Openings

**AusTender search strategy:**

1. Log in to austender.gov.au
2. Set up saved searches for keywords: "restoration", "remediation", "water damage", "mould", "cleaning and restoration", "building maintenance"
3. Filter by category: "Building Construction and Support and Maintenance and Repair Services"
4. Enable email notifications for new opportunities

**Response tips:**

- Answer every question in the evaluation criteria — skipping questions results in automatic exclusion
- Provide evidence, not assertions ("Our team holds 12 active IICRC certifications" vs "We are highly qualified")
- Include case studies that match the panel's scope
- Submit before the deadline — late submissions are never accepted

### Step 4: Panel Interview / Presentation

Some government panels require a presentation or interview. Prepare for:

- **Technical questions** — "Describe your approach to a Category 3 water loss in an occupied school"
- **WHS scenarios** — "How would you manage asbestos discovery during a restoration project?"
- **Capacity questions** — "Can you mobilise a team to a regional site within 24 hours?"
- **Quality assurance** — "How do you document moisture readings and drying progress?"

**Pro tip:** Bring your IICRC credentials, CEC records, and equipment inventory as printed supporting documents.

## Chapter 5: Worked Example — What Panel Readiness Looks Like

_The scenario below is illustrative. It is a composite example of the panel-readiness path, not an account of a specific CARSI client, and the figures are indicative only._

### An Illustrative Mid-Sized Restoration Business

**Company profile:**

- Location: South-East Queensland
- Staff: 14 (8 technicians, 3 project managers, 3 admin)
- Established restoration business with strong commercial and insurance work

**The challenge:**

The business has been operating for several years with steady commercial and insurance work but zero government contracts. The owner knows government panels offer stable, high-value work but earlier applications went nowhere — the experience was there, but the documented evidence wasn't.

**What a panel-ready business changes:**

1. **Closes the certification gap** — Technicians who need IICRC certification book it through an IICRC-approved school and examination. Everyone who already holds a certification keeps it current with IICRC CEC Accredited courses through CARSI, so every credential on the capability statement is active and auditable.

2. **Builds a WHS management system** — Engages a WHS consultant to formalise existing procedures into a documented management system, with SWMS for all high-risk activities.

3. **Upgrades insurance** — Increases public liability to the $20M government standard and adds pollution liability cover for mould and hazmat work.

4. **Creates a professional capability statement** — A concise document with an IICRC certification table, equipment inventory, and project case studies with measurable outcomes.

5. **Applies systematically** — Registers on AusTender, QTenders, and local council procurement platforms. Sets up keyword alerts. Responds to every relevant panel opening.

**Why this works:**

Panel evaluators score documented evidence, not assertions. A certification table where every entry is verifiable, current, and backed by CEC records answers the competency criteria directly — which is precisely where under-documented applications fall down. Businesses that make these changes put themselves in a position to compete credibly for state and council panel positions, and the online, self-paced format of CEC training means the upskilling happens without pulling technicians off billable work.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Government panels are accessible** — They're not reserved for large companies. Many panels specifically target SMEs and regional businesses.
2. **IICRC certifications are the entry ticket** — Without documented competency, your application is unlikely to succeed regardless of your experience.
3. **WHS compliance is non-negotiable** — Government agencies audit WHS records. Your system must be documented, current, and evidence-based.
4. **Start with your state** — Local and state government panels are generally easier to access than federal panels. Build your track record locally first.
5. **Online CEC training removes a major barrier** — CARSI's 24/7, self-paced IICRC CEC Accredited courses mean your team can keep certifications current without losing billable days.
