COURSE OVERVIEW

Professional glass cleaning is a practical skill that restoration and cleaning technicians are frequently called on to perform following water damage events, construction works, renovation projects, and routine commercial cleaning contracts. Poorly executed glass cleaning produces streaks, smearing, and scratching — all of which are avoidable with correct technique, appropriate tools, and properly prepared cleaning solutions.

This course covers the complete glass cleaning workflow: pre-job planning and preparation, the cleaning process itself, supplementary video guidance, bonus content on window track cleaning, and a knowledge assessment. Content is aligned with professional cleaning standards relevant to the Australian market.

Topics covered: site assessment and equipment preparation, glass cleaning chemistry and technique, practical video demonstration reference, window track cleaning procedures, and a quiz to confirm knowledge retention.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MODULE 1: Plan and Prepare

Planning and preparation before picking up a squeegee determines the quality of the finished result. Experienced glass cleaners spend proportionally more time on preparation than on cleaning — because errors in preparation produce results that no amount of cleaning technique can fix.

ASSESS THE GLASS BEFORE TOUCHING IT

Walk the full extent of the glass to be cleaned before opening any equipment. Identify:
- Type of glass: standard annealed, toughened, laminated, heritage / heritage-pattern, frosted, tinted, or coated (Low-E, solar control). Coated glass has specific chemical and abrasive restrictions — using the wrong product will strip the coating permanently.
- Condition: scratches, chips, mineral deposits (calcium and silica scale from hard water or sprinkler overspray), paint overspray, adhesive residue, silicone contamination, or oxidised seals. Identify these before cleaning and document them with photographs — pre-existing damage noted before work begins cannot be attributed to your work.
- Access requirements: working at height (ladder, elevated work platform), working over water features or garden beds (requires drop sheet management), or working in occupied buildings where overspray or water runoff must be controlled.
- Frame material: aluminium, uPVC, timber, or powder-coated steel. Each has chemical compatibility requirements. Aggressive alkaline solutions can strip powder coating and stain aluminium frames if not rinsed promptly.

EQUIPMENT CHECKLIST

Confirm before arriving on site that you have:
- Squeegee (correct channel width for the glass — a squeegee narrower than the glass panel requires multiple passes and leaves overlap marks)
- Squeegee rubber in good condition (no nicks, cuts, or hardened sections — replace squeegee rubber every 4 to 8 hours of active use)
- Scrubber / applicator sleeve (microfibre or lambswool — matched to the glass condition)
- Bucket with appropriate solution (diluted professional glass cleaning concentrate or a low-residue alternative — avoid detergents that leave surfactant residue)
- Scrim cloths or lint-free detailing cloths for edges and corners
- Razor blade scraper in a safety holder (for paint, adhesive, or hard deposits — only on untreated, uncoated glass)
- Extension pole matched to the window height
- Drop sheet or barrier to protect frames and surfaces below

SOLUTION PREPARATION

Mix glass cleaning solution at the manufacturer's specified dilution. In hot Australian conditions, slightly increasing the concentration prevents the solution from flashing off (drying) before the squeegee pass is complete. Cold water increases working time in summer. Never use neat washing-up detergent — the surfactants leave a residue that attracts dirt and causes rapid re-soiling.

Reference: Australian Window Association — Glass and Glazing Standards | Safe Work Australia — Working at Heights Code of Practice

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MODULE 2: Clean the Glass

Correct glass cleaning technique eliminates streaks, prevents re-soiling, and protects the glass surface and frame. There is a specific sequence — follow it on every panel.

STANDARD CLEANING SEQUENCE

Step 1 — Pre-rinse. For exterior glass with significant dust or grit, rinse the surface with clean water before applying cleaning solution. Wiping a gritty glass surface with a scrubber without pre-rinsing embeds particles in the scrubber and scratches the glass.

Step 2 — Apply solution. Saturate the scrubber / applicator sleeve with solution and apply to the full glass surface using overlapping horizontal strokes. Ensure complete coverage including edges and corners — dry edges cause streaks as the squeegee drags solvent residue from the wet centre across a dry border.

Step 3 — Agitate. For normally soiled glass, the application stroke provides sufficient agitation. For glass with heavier soiling (construction dust, cooking grease, or insect residue), use circular scrubbing strokes before the squeegee pass.

Step 4 — Squeegee. Start at the top corner of the panel and pull the squeegee across the top of the glass in one controlled stroke, angled slightly downward to direct water away from the frame at the leading edge. Wipe the squeegee rubber after each stroke with a scrim cloth to remove accumulated water and soil — a dirty rubber re-deposits contamination on the next stroke. Work downward in overlapping horizontal bands until the full panel is completed.

Step 5 — Detail edges. Use a clean, dry scrim or lint-free cloth to wipe any remaining water from the frame perimeter, corners, and the bottom of the panel. This prevents water from running back onto the clean glass from the frame or sill.

Step 6 — Inspect. Step back and inspect the panel from an angle at which any streaks or missed areas will be visible. Touch up as required before moving to the next panel.

COMMON ERRORS AND HOW TO AVOID THEM

Streaks running from top to bottom: squeegee rubber is nicked or dirty. Replace or clean the rubber.
Streaks at the sides: insufficient solution applied to edges. Saturate edges fully before squeegeeing.
Smearing across the full panel: solution is contaminated with old residue from the bucket. Change the water and solution after every 4 to 6 panels or when the water becomes visibly dirty.
Mineral deposit marks remain after cleaning: standard cleaning solution does not remove calcium or silica scale. Use a proprietary scale remover or diluted acidic cleaner appropriate for the glass type (test in an inconspicuous area first).

Reference: Australian Window Association | IICRC S100 (surface cleaning principles) | AS 1288 (Glass in Buildings)

MODULE 4: BONUS — Window Tracks

Window tracks are among the most neglected surfaces in residential and commercial cleaning — and among the most frequently complained about by clients who notice them after the glass has been cleaned. Leaving dirty tracks adjacent to clean glass produces an incomplete result and a dissatisfied client. Cleaning tracks correctly adds minutes to a job and significantly improves the client's perception of quality.

WHY TRACKS ACCUMULATE HEAVY SOILING

Window tracks collect: construction dust and concrete residue (in new builds and renovation sites), insect debris, mould growth in humid coastal climates, calcium scale from condensation and humidity, paint overspray from building works, and leaf litter and organic material in external tracks. The combination of fine particles, moisture, and organic matter in a confined channel creates soiling that resists standard spray-and-wipe cleaning.

TRACK CLEANING PROCESS

Step 1 — Dry removal first. Use a stiff, narrow brush or a dedicated track cleaning brush to loosen dry debris from the full length of the track before applying any liquid. Applying liquid to dry compacted debris turns it into a paste that is harder to remove and may stain the track surface.

Step 2 — Vacuum. A crevice tool on a small vacuum removes the loosened dry material efficiently. This step is worth doing — skipping it means the debris gets pushed into the cleaning cloth and redeposited along the track.

Step 3 — Apply cleaning solution. For standard soiling, diluted all-purpose cleaner applied with a small brush or spray is adequate. For calcium or mineral deposits, an acidic descaling solution (tested for compatibility with the track material — aluminium reacts with strong acids) provides faster results. For mould in tracks, a diluted disinfectant or mould-specific treatment is required.

Step 4 — Agitate with a detail brush. A toothbrush-style detail brush or a dedicated track cleaning tool works the solution into corners and the full depth of the channel. Work along the full length of the track in short scrubbing strokes.

Step 5 — Wipe out. Use a cloth folded to fit the track width — not a folded cloth pressed flat across the track opening — to draw soiling out of the channel. Work from one end of the track to the other in a single direction. Refold the cloth to a clean surface after each pass.

Step 6 — Final inspection and wipe. Run a clean, damp cloth through the track to remove any residual cleaning product, then a dry cloth to remove moisture.

ROLLERS AND MECHANISM CHECK

While cleaning tracks, check that rollers (on sliding windows and doors) are moving freely and are not worn or damaged. Alert the client to any worn or seized rollers — this is a value-add observation that clients appreciate and that positions you as a thorough professional.

Reference: Australian Window Association — Window maintenance guidelines

COURSE OVERVIEW

Residential duct cleaning is a growing service category within the Australian home services sector. Dirty ductwork degrades indoor air quality, reduces HVAC system efficiency, and — in the case of dryer duct systems — creates a genuine fire hazard. This course introduces the business context, technical knowledge, and equipment operation skills required to provide professional residential duct cleaning services.

Topics covered: the commercial opportunity in residential duct cleaning, residential HVAC system architecture, the two primary cleaning methodologies (contact and negative pressure), the specific equipment systems used in each methodology, and the end-to-end cleaning process.

This course is in Draft status. Content reflects the current module framework and is subject to review and supplementation with practical field photography and video resources prior to final publication.

Standards applied: AIRAH (Australian Institute of Refrigeration, Air Conditioning and Heating) technical guidelines, AS/NZS 3823 (performance of air conditioners and heat pumps), Safe Work Australia Model WHS Regulations, state fire safety regulations applicable to dryer duct systems.

MODULE 1: The Business of Residential Cleaning

MARKET CONTEXT

Residential duct cleaning in Australia operates in an underserved market. The majority of Australian homeowners with ducted HVAC systems have never had their ductwork professionally cleaned — many are unaware the service exists. Industry data consistently shows that duct cleaning businesses with professional presentation, clear service descriptions, and the ability to demonstrate before-and-after results grow quickly through referrals.

The residential duct cleaning service sits alongside water damage restoration, carpet cleaning, and HVAC maintenance as a complementary service that restoration and cleaning businesses can add to their existing capabilities with targeted equipment investment.

COMMERCIAL OPPORTUNITY

Primary revenue streams in residential duct cleaning:
- Standard duct system cleaning (supply and return air ducts, grilles, and register cleaning)
- Dryer duct cleaning (fire safety service — independently marketed to homeowners, real estate agents, and property managers)
- Sanitisation and antimicrobial treatment of duct systems (post-mould, post-renovation, or post-pest-infestation events)
- Pre-occupancy duct cleaning for newly purchased or rental properties (increasingly requested by property managers)
- Annual maintenance programmes (recurring revenue — subscription or reminder-based re-booking)

PRICING STRUCTURE

Residential duct cleaning is priced per system, per zone, or by component count (number of vents). Research local market rates before establishing pricing. Pricing variables include: number of vents, system type (ducted gas, ducted reverse cycle, evaporative), accessibility of the duct system (roof space, subfloor, or wall-mounted), and the extent of contamination.

BUSINESS REQUIREMENTS

Operating a residential duct cleaning business in Australia requires: appropriate insurance (public liability minimum $10 million, consider product liability cover for sanitisation services), a reliable late-model vehicle capable of carrying equipment, professional presentation and uniforms, a booking system capable of managing residential appointments, and an online presence with before-and-after photography.

Reference: AIRAH | Australian Home Service Association | Safe Work Australia

MODULE 2: Understanding Residential HVAC Systems

SYSTEM TYPES

Residential duct cleaning technicians encounter three primary HVAC system types:

Ducted reverse cycle (refrigerant-based): the most common system in southern Australian states. A central air handling unit (typically in the roof space or ceiling cavity) distributes conditioned air through insulated flexible or rigid ductwork to supply registers throughout the home. Return air is drawn back to the unit through a central return air grille (usually a ceiling-mounted grille in a central hall or living area) or through return air ducts.

Ducted gas heating with or without evaporative cooling: common in Victoria and South Australia. The gas furnace unit is typically in the roof space. Supply ducts are similar to reverse cycle systems. Evaporative coolers may be integrated or operate through separate ductwork.

Evaporative cooling systems: common in dry inland Australian climates (Western Australia, South Australia, Victoria). A roof-mounted evaporative unit draws outside air through water-saturated pads and distributes cooled air through ductwork to ceiling registers. These systems operate with windows and doors slightly open to allow air flow through the home.

SYSTEM COMPONENTS RELEVANT TO CLEANING

Air handling unit (AHU): the central unit containing the fan, heat exchanger or evaporator coil, and filter. The AHU interior and coil require cleaning as part of a comprehensive duct cleaning service. Access panels on the unit allow inspection and cleaning of the fan blades and coil face.

Supply ductwork: insulated flexible ducts (Flexduct) or rigid sheet metal ducts running from the AHU through the roof space to each register. Flexible duct is susceptible to kinking, collapse, and rodent damage — inspect for condition while cleaning.

Return air system: the pathway through which room air is drawn back to the AHU. This may be a single large central grille and return air plenum, or a dedicated return air duct network.

Registers and grilles: the visible supply and return air outlets in ceilings, walls, or floors. Registers accumulate dust, pet hair, and biological material on their face and in the first 300mm of the duct immediately behind them — often the most heavily contaminated location in the system.

Dryer duct: a separate, dedicated duct from a clothes dryer to the exterior of the building. Not part of the HVAC system but a critical fire-safety cleaning service offered by duct cleaning businesses.

Reference: AIRAH Technical Handbook | Manufacturer installation guides for common Australian residential HVAC brands

MODULE 3: Contact Vs. Negative Pressure Duct Cleaning

TWO PRIMARY APPROACHES

Professional residential duct cleaning is performed using one of two methodologies: contact cleaning and negative pressure cleaning. Understanding the distinction between these methods allows you to select the right approach for each system and client situation.

CONTACT CLEANING

Contact cleaning uses mechanical tools — rotating brushes, compressed air whips, or air-driven agitation tools — to physically dislodge contamination from duct surfaces. The dislodged material is collected by the tool (if the tool has a debris collection function), vacuumed out through the register opening, or pushed toward a collection point within the duct system.

Contact cleaning tools are inserted through the register openings or access points and directed through the ductwork. The technician has direct mechanical contact with the duct surface via the tool.

When contact cleaning is appropriate:
- Systems with moderate to heavy dust accumulation
- Flexible ductwork (contact tools must be selected and operated carefully to avoid puncturing flexible duct liner)
- Systems where access for a collection vacuum at the AHU end of the duct is limited
- Smaller residential systems where the additional equipment of a full negative pressure setup is not warranted

NEGATIVE PRESSURE CLEANING

Negative pressure cleaning connects a high-powered vacuum collection unit to the duct system — typically at the AHU or a main trunk duct — and creates negative pressure (suction) throughout the system. Agitation tools (brushes, air whips, contact tools) are then used at each register to dislodge contamination, which the negative pressure airflow carries toward and into the vacuum collection unit.

Negative pressure is more effective at capturing dislodged material before it re-settles and provides a higher standard of cleaning for systems with significant contamination.

When negative pressure cleaning is appropriate:
- Heavily contaminated systems
- Systems where the AHU or trunk duct is accessible for vacuum connection
- Post-mould remediation duct cleaning where containment of dislodged material is required
- Commercial or premium residential services requiring a verifiable high-standard result

COMPARISON SUMMARY

Contact cleaning: lower equipment cost, faster setup, effective for standard residential maintenance cleaning, adequate for most residential jobs.

Negative pressure cleaning: higher equipment investment, more complex setup, superior contamination capture, required for heavily contaminated systems and post-remediation work.

Many duct cleaning businesses use both methods in combination — negative pressure vacuum for collection combined with contact agitation tools at each register.

Reference: AIRAH | NADCA (National Air Duct Cleaners Association) standard ACR (Assessment, Cleaning and Restoration of HVAC Systems)

MODULE 4: Negative Air Duct Cleaning Systems

SYSTEM OVERVIEW

A negative air duct cleaning system consists of a high-powered vacuum unit connected to the HVAC duct system to create sustained negative pressure throughout the duct network during the cleaning process. As agitation tools dislodge contamination from duct surfaces at each register, the negative pressure airflow carries the material toward the collection unit.

EQUIPMENT COMPONENTS

Collection vacuum unit: a truck-mounted or trailer-mounted high-powered vacuum (typically diesel or petrol-engine driven) with HEPA filtration to ensure that dislodged material is captured rather than exhausted back into the environment. The unit connects to the duct system via a flexible collection hose. Engine-driven units provide significantly higher airflow and suction than portable electric units and are the industry standard for negative pressure residential duct cleaning.

Collection hose and system connection: a large-diameter (typically 200mm to 300mm) flexible hose connects the collection unit to the duct system. Connection is made at the AHU access panel, at the return air plenum, or through a purpose-drilled access hole in the main trunk duct. The connection must be airtight to maintain negative pressure throughout the system.

Register sealing tools: foam plugs, magnetic covers, or adhesive covers are used to seal registers that are not actively being cleaned. Sealing registers concentrates the negative pressure in the duct section being agitated and prevents dislodged material from exiting through unsealed registers into the home.

Agitation tools used in combination with negative pressure: contact brushes inserted through register openings, air whips (flexible hoses with nozzle ends that spin under compressed air pressure), and hand-held air guns for direct register cleaning.

SETUP PROCEDURE

1. Protect the work area with floor coverings at each register and at the AHU.
2. Connect the collection hose to the duct system at the AHU or main trunk access point.
3. Seal all supply and return registers with register covers — leave one register unsealed to maintain a controlled airflow path.
4. Start the collection vacuum unit and confirm negative pressure is established (a visual indicator is that register covers are pulled flat against the register face by the suction).
5. Begin agitation at the register furthest from the collection point, working systematically back toward the collection connection.
6. Unseal each register only while agitating that register — reseal before moving to the next.

Reference: NADCA ACR Standard | AIRAH | Equipment manufacturer operating manuals

MODULE 5: The Dust Master Contact Cleaning System

SYSTEM OVERVIEW

The Dust Master is a contact duct cleaning system designed for the residential market. It uses a rotating brush mechanism to physically agitate and remove contamination from duct surfaces, with a built-in vacuum collection function that captures dislodged material at the point of agitation.

EQUIPMENT COMPONENTS

The Dust Master system includes: a portable electric drive unit, a range of flexible shaft extensions that allow the rotating brush to reach throughout duct runs from a single register access point, interchangeable brush heads in multiple sizes to match standard Australian residential duct diameters (typically 150mm to 250mm diameter flexible duct), and an integrated or companion vacuum for debris collection.

SETUP AND OPERATION

Setup: connect the drive unit to power, attach the appropriate brush head and shaft length for the duct diameter and estimated run length, and position the vacuum collection at the register opening before beginning rotation.

Operation: insert the rotating brush into the register and advance it through the duct run using the flexible shaft. Apply light forward pressure — the rotating brush cleans on both the forward pass and the withdrawal. Advance and withdraw the brush in controlled strokes, allowing the brush to contact the full circumference of the duct interior. Do not force the brush through bends or restrictions — excessive force can collapse flexible duct or damage duct connections.

Brush selection: use a brush diameter that matches the duct interior diameter — a brush that is too small will miss sections of the duct surface; a brush that is too large will struggle through bends and may cause duct damage. When the duct diameter is uncertain, start with a smaller brush and size up once the duct interior has been confirmed.

APPLICATIONS

The Dust Master contact system is well-suited to: standard residential flexible duct systems, dryer duct cleaning (using appropriate brush sizes), moderate soiling levels where full negative pressure setup is not required, and single-technician residential service operations.

Limitation: the Dust Master captures material at the agitation point but does not prevent re-settling of finer particles dislodged further into the duct system. For heavily contaminated systems, combine with a downstream negative pressure collection unit for superior results.

Reference: Dust Master equipment manufacturer documentation | AIRAH | Safe Work Australia

MODULE 6: Vent Vac Dryer Duct Cleaning System

SYSTEM OVERVIEW

The Vent Vac system is specifically designed for residential dryer duct cleaning — the removal of lint accumulation from the duct connecting a residential clothes dryer to the building exterior. Dryer duct lint accumulation is the leading cause of residential dryer-related fires in Australia. A blocked or partially blocked dryer duct causes lint to build up, increases drying times (increasing energy consumption), stresses the dryer motor, and in the worst case creates conditions for a duct fire.

WHY DRYER DUCT CLEANING IS A FIRE SAFETY SERVICE

The Australian Building Codes Board and state fire authorities identify lint accumulation in dryer exhaust ducts as a fire hazard. Many home insurance policies require dryer ducts to be maintained in a clean condition — a policy detail that most homeowners are unaware of until a fire occurs and a claim is disputed. Positioning dryer duct cleaning as a fire safety and insurance compliance service (not just a cleaning service) is both accurate and commercially effective.

SYSTEM COMPONENTS

The Vent Vac system includes: a dedicated electric or compressed-air-driven brush rotation unit, flexible shaft sections that allow the system to navigate the bends common in residential dryer duct runs, dryer-specific brush heads sized for standard Australian dryer duct diameters (typically 100mm to 125mm), and a vacuum collection attachment for lint capture.

OPERATION

Step 1 — Disconnect the dryer from the duct at the dryer connection point (behind the dryer, typically a 100mm or 125mm flexible duct clamp connection).

Step 2 — Insert the Vent Vac brush from the dryer connection end of the duct and advance toward the exterior termination point. Work in controlled strokes, advancing and withdrawing the brush to ensure full contact with the duct interior.

Step 3 — At the exterior end, clean the exterior termination cap (louver or flap type) — this is frequently blocked by lint and insect nesting material.

Step 4 — Vacuum the duct interior from both ends to remove dislodged lint.

Step 5 — Reconnect the dryer and run a short cycle to confirm airflow is restored and the exterior flap is operating correctly.

Step 6 — Photograph the lint collected and the clear duct at completion for the client's documentation.

Indicator of blockage severity: if a strong hand over the exterior vent termination during dryer operation detects minimal airflow, the duct is significantly restricted and requires immediate cleaning. Most homeowners have never checked this.

Reference: State fire authority guidelines on dryer duct safety | ABCB | Vent Vac manufacturer documentation

MODULE 7: Cobra Power Brush Duct Cleaning System

SYSTEM OVERVIEW

The Cobra Power Brush system is a professional-grade contact cleaning system designed for both residential and light commercial duct cleaning. It uses a high-speed rotating brush driven by a portable electric or pneumatic drive unit, with a modular flexible shaft system that allows the brush to navigate through complex duct configurations including multiple bends and transitions.

EQUIPMENT COMPONENTS

Drive unit: a portable electric or pneumatic drive unit that rotates the flexible shaft and brush head. Electric versions are suitable for standard residential applications. Pneumatic versions require a compressor supply but offer higher torque for use in larger or more heavily contaminated systems.

Flexible shaft and extensions: the Cobra system uses interlocking flexible shaft sections that can be assembled to reach the full length of most residential duct runs from a single access point. Sections are available in standard lengths and connect with a secure locking mechanism to prevent disassembly inside the duct.

Brush heads: multiple brush head options are available including nylon bristle brushes for standard dust and lint removal, polypropylene stiff-bristle brushes for compacted contamination, and combination brush heads. Select the brush head and diameter matched to the duct material — flexible duct requires softer brush bristles than rigid sheet metal duct to avoid liner damage.

OPERATION

1. Assemble the shaft to the estimated duct run length before inserting — adding shaft sections once partially inserted is awkward and risks losing shaft sections in the duct.
2. Insert the brush head and advance with the drive unit running. Let the brush do the work — forced advancement damages flexible duct.
3. Use a withdrawal-and-advance technique at each section of ductwork to ensure complete contact cleaning of the duct wall.
4. Position a vacuum collection at the register opening or at the AHU connection point to capture dislodged material.
5. After completing each duct run, withdraw the full shaft, inspect the brush for material accumulation, and clean before advancing to the next register.

APPLICATIONS

The Cobra Power Brush system is suitable for: complete residential HVAC duct system cleaning, dryer duct cleaning (with appropriate brush sizes), roof space and subfloor duct runs with multiple bends, and post-renovation duct cleaning where construction dust has accumulated in the system.

MODULE 8: The Residential Duct Cleaning Process

COMPLETE WORKFLOW — JOB TO JOB

This module brings together all preceding content into the complete residential duct cleaning process — from client arrival through to job completion and documentation.

STEP 1 — ARRIVAL AND SITE SETUP

Introduce yourself to the client and confirm the scope of work. Walk through the home to identify register locations, confirm system type and layout, and locate the AHU (typically in the roof space, ceiling cavity, or a dedicated plant cupboard). Lay protective floor covers at each register access point and at the AHU access. Confirm power availability and placement for equipment.

STEP 2 — SYSTEM ASSESSMENT

Before cleaning begins, assess the system condition. Inspect accessible duct sections for damage, disconnections, or significant moisture staining (indicating prior water intrusion). Photograph the AHU filter (record filter condition — a heavily loaded filter indicates long cleaning intervals and sets client expectations for the system's contamination level). Remove and set aside the filter for cleaning or disposal and replacement. Inspect the AHU interior (coil face and fan blades) and photograph.

STEP 3 — EQUIPMENT CONNECTION AND SETUP

Connect the collection vacuum at the AHU or main trunk access point (for negative pressure method), or prepare the contact cleaning equipment at the first register (for contact method). Seal all registers except the one being actively cleaned.

STEP 4 — CLEANING — REGISTER BY REGISTER

Work systematically through the home register by register. For each register:
- Remove the register face / grille and clean with a brush or compressed air, then wash and dry
- Insert the cleaning tool and clean the duct run from the register to the extent of the tool's reach
- Vacuum at the register opening to capture dislodged material
- Reinstall the clean register
- Move to the next register and repeat

Clean the return air system — remove, clean, and reinstall the return air grille, and clean the return air duct or plenum behind the grille.

STEP 5 — AHU CLEANING

Clean the AHU interior using appropriate brushes and a HEPA-filtered vacuum. Clean fan blades with a damp cloth. Clean the coil face using a soft brush — do not use pressure water or aggressive cleaning solutions on the coil without specific manufacturer guidance. Install a new or cleaned filter before closing the AHU.

STEP 6 — SYSTEM RESTART AND CONFIRMATION

Restore all equipment connections and register covers. Restart the HVAC system and confirm all registers are delivering airflow. Walk through the home with the client to confirm the system is operating normally.

STEP 7 — DOCUMENTATION AND CLOSE-OUT

Provide the client with a written completion record including: date of service, number of registers cleaned, pre-cleaning photographs, post-cleaning photographs, filter condition before and after, any observations about system condition (damaged duct sections, disconnected duct runs, coil contamination beyond the scope of standard cleaning), and recommended next service interval.

Collect payment and provide a receipt. Request a review if the client is satisfied — duct cleaning businesses grow primarily through referral and review-driven discovery.

Reference: AIRAH | NADCA ACR Standard | Safe Work Australia | State fire authority requirements for dryer duct services

RESOURCE: Insurance Adjusters and Their Roles
Resource Number: #23
Price: Free
Status: Published
Modules: None

RESOURCE OVERVIEW

Insurance adjusters are the professional gatekeepers of the claim approval and payment process in Australian property insurance. For restoration contractors, understanding who adjusters are, what they do, how they are measured, and how to work with them effectively is a direct commercial capability — it determines how quickly your scopes get approved, how completely your invoices get paid, and whether you get referred back to the same insurer's panel jobs.

WHAT THIS RESOURCE COVERS

This resource provides a practical overview of the insurance adjuster's role, their professional context, and the specific expectations they have of restoration contractors. It is a professional development resource — not a formal course with assessed modules — but the knowledge it provides has direct, measurable commercial impact.

WHO INSURANCE ADJUSTERS ARE

Insurance adjusters (also called loss adjusters or claims assessors) are professionals engaged by insurers to investigate, assess, and quantify property damage claims. In Australia, adjusters operate under two primary models:

Staff adjusters are employees of the insuring company. They handle claims directly on behalf of their employer-insurer. Their role includes attending sites, reviewing contractor scopes, approving or disputing claim costs, and managing the overall claim lifecycle.

Independent loss adjusters are independent professionals engaged by insurers on a contract basis — particularly for larger, more complex, or higher-value claims. They are bound by professional standards and codes of conduct and are expected to assess claims objectively on behalf of the insurer. The Australian Institute of Loss Adjusters (AILA) is the professional body for independent adjusters in Australia.

WHAT ADJUSTERS DO AND HOW THEY WORK

An adjuster's primary function is to determine whether a claim is covered under the policy, and if so, to quantify the cost of the loss to a standard consistent with the policy's indemnity obligations. Their workflow on a water damage claim typically includes:
- Reviewing the claim lodgement to confirm coverage in principle
- Attending the site (for larger claims) or conducting a desktop review (for smaller claims) to assess damage extent
- Reviewing the restoration contractor's scope of works and estimate for completeness, accuracy, and compliance with the applicable repair standard
- Negotiating scope items where the insurer's position differs from the contractor's
- Authorising payment of approved invoices

HOW RESTORATION CONTRACTORS SHOULD WORK WITH ADJUSTERS

The restoration contractor-adjuster relationship works most effectively when both parties communicate clearly, professionally, and promptly. Practical guidance:

Respond to adjuster enquiries on the same business day. Adjusters manage multiple claims simultaneously and value contractors who are responsive. A contractor who is slow to respond to adjuster communications is quickly deprioritised for panel referrals.

Submit complete, well-documented scopes on first lodgement. A scope that requires multiple revision cycles wastes the adjuster's time and delays your payment. The discipline covered in the CARSI Water Damage Estimating course directly supports this outcome.

Document everything and make your documentation accessible to the adjuster promptly on request. Adjusters cannot approve what they cannot verify. Photographs, moisture readings, drying logs, and clearance reports that are organised and promptly available accelerate the claim settlement cycle.

When disputing an adjuster's scope decision, provide factual evidence for your position — not advocacy. An adjuster who receives a well-evidenced explanation of why a scope item is required will reconsider. An adjuster who receives a general complaint without supporting evidence will not.

Understand that adjusters have targets and KPIs. Claims cycle time is a metric that most adjusters are measured against. A restoration contractor who helps an adjuster close claims efficiently — through responsive communication, accurate first-submission scopes, and prompt clearance documentation — becomes a preferred partner, not just a panel participant.

REGULATORY AND PROFESSIONAL CONTEXT

Insurance adjusters in Australia operate under the Insurance Council of Australia's General Insurance Code of Practice, which sets standards for claims handling timeliness, communication, and dispute resolution. The Code gives policyholders specific rights including the right to request an internal review and, if unresolved, an external dispute resolution process through the Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA).

As a restoration contractor, understanding this framework helps you advise clients who are experiencing claim handling difficulties — a service that positions you as a knowledgeable professional rather than simply a trade contractor.

Reference: Australian Institute of Loss Adjusters (AILA) | Insurance Council of Australia — General Insurance Code of Practice | Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA) | IICRC S500 (for scope standards referenced by adjusters)
